A MarketWatch analysis identified eight spending patterns that drain retirement accounts faster than most people expect. The numbers back it up: according to the Employee Benefit Research Institute (EBRI), 40.6% of U.S. households headed by someone aged 35 to 64 are projected to run short of money in retirement. That number jumps when you factor in healthcare costs.

Here are the eight mistakes -- and the actual data behind each one.

## The 8 Retirement Money Drains

### 1. Underestimating Healthcare Costs

Fidelity's 2025 Retiree Health Care Cost Estimate puts the average 65-year-old couple's lifetime healthcare spending at $365,000 -- and that excludes long-term care. Medicare Part B premiums alone hit $185 per month in 2025, up from $170.10 in 2024.

### 2. Withdrawing Too Much, Too Fast

The traditional "4% rule" assumed a 30-year retirement. But the Society of Actuaries reports that a 65-year-old man has a 35% chance of living past 90 and a 65-year-old woman has a 46% chance. Drawing down 5-6% annually -- as many retirees do -- can drain a $500,000 portfolio in under 15 years.

### 3. Ignoring Inflation's Compound Effect

The Bureau of Labor Statistics CPI data shows cumulative inflation of 22.5% from January 2020 to December 2025. A dollar that bought a full grocery run in 2020 now covers about 78 cents worth of food. Retirees on fixed incomes feel this harder than workers who get raises.

### 4. Carrying Debt Into Retirement

The Federal Reserve's Survey of Consumer Finances shows 38% of households headed by someone 65-74 still carry mortgage debt, with a median balance of $110,000. Add credit cards and auto loans, and monthly obligations eat into limited income fast.

### 5. Paying High Investment Fees

A 1% annual fee on a $400,000 portfolio costs $4,000 per year. Over 20 years of retirement, fee drag can consume $80,000 or more of your savings -- money that should be funding your daily life.

### 6. Not Adjusting Spending After Retirement

The Bureau of Labor Statistics Consumer Expenditure Survey shows households aged 65+ spend an average of $57,818 per year. Many retirees maintain their working-years spending for the first 3-5 years, then face painful cutbacks when the math catches up.

### 7. Over-Relying on Social Security

The Social Security Administration reports the average retired worker benefit at $1,976 per month as of January 2025. That is $23,712 per year -- below the federal poverty line for a two-person household in many high-cost states.

### 8. Keeping Everything in One Asset Class

Retirees with 100% stock portfolios saw 34% losses during the 2008 crash. Those with 100% bonds faced negative real returns when inflation spiked in 2021-2023. Diversification across asset classes -- including hard assets like precious metals and real estate -- reduces the risk that any single downturn wipes out your income.

## What Actually Works

The retirees who avoid these traps share a few habits:

- **They know their numbers.** They track monthly spending against actual income, not projections.
- **They hold a mix of assets.** Stocks for growth, bonds for income, and hard assets like gold for purchasing-power protection. The World Gold Council reports gold returned 12.8% annually over the five years ending December 2025.
- **They plan for 30+ years.** With life expectancy rising, a 60-year-old today needs to plan for spending through age 92 or beyond.
- **They keep two years of expenses in cash or near-cash.** This prevents forced selling during market downturns.

## The Bottom Line

Retirement mistakes are not about willpower. They stem from planning gaps that compound over decades. The data from EBRI, the SSA, Fidelity, and the BLS all point the same direction: most Americans need more diversified income sources and lower withdrawal rates than they currently plan for.
