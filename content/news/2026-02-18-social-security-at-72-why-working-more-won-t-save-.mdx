A 72-year-old MarketWatch reader who started claiming Social Security at 69 recently asked whether working part-time could increase his monthly check. It's a reasonable question, and the answer involves understanding how the SSA's benefit formula actually works after you've started collecting.

The short version: maybe, but probably not by much. Here's the detailed breakdown.

## How Social Security Recalculates After You Claim

Social Security uses your **highest 35 years of indexed earnings** to calculate your benefit. The SSA automatically reviews your earnings record each year to check whether new earnings would replace a lower year in your top 35. If they do, your benefit gets recalculated upward.

Here's how the process works:

1. The SSA collects your new earnings for the year (reported by your employer on W-2 or by you on Schedule SE)
2. They compare the new earnings against your lowest year in the current top 35
3. If the new year is higher, it replaces the low year and your AIME (Average Indexed Monthly Earnings) increases
4. The recalculation happens automatically, usually in October of the following year

**Key detail:** For workers already past age 60, the SSA does not index new earnings for inflation. They enter the formula at face value. But earnings from your 20s and 30s are indexed upward significantly. So part-time wages at 72 compete against inflation-adjusted earnings from decades ago.

## When Working at 72 Actually Helps

Working after claiming helps your benefit primarily in two scenarios:

| Situation | Likely Impact |
|---|---|
| You have fewer than 35 years of earnings | New earnings replace $0 years, meaningful increase |
| Some of your 35 years had very low earnings | New earnings may replace those years, modest increase |
| You worked 35+ years with solid earnings throughout | Part-time wages likely don't replace any existing year, minimal impact |

For someone who worked steadily from their 20s through their late 60s, part-time income at 72 would need to exceed their lowest indexed year to make any difference. For a worker whose lowest top-35 year (after indexing) was $25,000, a part-time job paying $15,000 wouldn't change the calculation at all.

## The Earnings Test No Longer Applies at 72

One piece of good news: the Social Security **retirement earnings test** only applies before full retirement age (currently 67). After FRA, you can earn unlimited income without any benefit reduction.

Before FRA, the SSA withholds **$1 in benefits for every $2 earned** above $22,320 (2024 limit). In the year you reach FRA, the formula changes to **$1 for every $3** above $59,520 (2024). After FRA, there's no withholding at all.

For a 72-year-old, this is a non-issue. Every dollar earned is kept, and any Social Security recalculation only goes up, never down.

## What the COLA Actually Covers

Social Security benefits receive an annual **Cost-of-Living Adjustment (COLA)** based on the Consumer Price Index for Urban Wage Earners and Clerical Workers (CPI-W). Here's how recent COLAs have compared to overall inflation:

| Year | COLA | CPI-U (all items) | Difference |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2022 | 5.9% | 8.0% | -2.1% |
| 2023 | 8.7% | 4.1% | +4.6% |
| 2024 | 3.2% | 2.9% | +0.3% |
| 2025 | 2.5% | TBD | TBD |

Source: SSA (COLA), BLS (CPI-U annual averages).

The COLA sometimes overshoots inflation, sometimes undershoots it. Over the long run, the Senior Citizens League (a nonpartisan advocacy group) calculated that Social Security benefits lost approximately **36% of their buying power** between 2000 and 2023, because the CPI-W doesn't adequately capture retiree spending on healthcare and housing.

## The Real Math on Social Security Income

Here's what the SSA's own data shows about current benefits:

- **Average retired worker benefit:** $1,907/month (January 2025)
- **Average couple (both collecting):** $3,033/month
- **Maximum benefit at age 67 (FRA):** $3,822/month
- **Maximum benefit at age 70:** $4,873/month

The BLS reports average annual expenses for 65-74 households at **$60,087**. Even the maximum benefit at age 70 ($58,476/year) barely covers average expenses, and most recipients receive far less than the maximum.

## Building Additional Income Sources

The Federal Reserve's 2022 Survey of Consumer Finances found that retirees with diversified income performed significantly better financially:

- **One income source (typically Social Security only):** Median household income of $22,000
- **Three or more sources:** Median household income exceeding $67,000

Practical options for strengthening retirement income alongside Social Security:

- **Roth conversions in lower-income years.** Converting traditional IRA funds to Roth while in a lower tax bracket creates tax-free income for future years and eliminates RMD requirements on converted amounts.
- **Catch-up contributions if still working.** Workers 50+ can contribute $8,000 to an IRA in 2025. Workers 60-63 get a "super catch-up" 401(k) contribution of $34,750 under SECURE 2.0.
- **Dividend and interest income.** Building a taxable portfolio that generates income outside of retirement accounts adds flexibility.
- **Physical assets with low stock correlation.** Gold returned an annualized 8.4% over 20 years ending December 2024 (World Gold Council), with a correlation to the S&P 500 of approximately 0.05. A self-directed IRA can hold IRS-approved gold and silver, providing diversification that doesn't depend on stock market performance.
