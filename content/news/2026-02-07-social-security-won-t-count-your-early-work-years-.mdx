A 69-year-old MarketWatch reader who started working as a dishwasher at age 13 recently discovered that Social Security wouldn't credit those early work years toward his benefit. He felt cheated. But the Social Security Administration's rules on this are specific, documented, and worth understanding if you've had a long working life.

Here's how the benefit formula actually works, why early work years might not help as much as you'd expect, and what you can do about it.

## How Social Security Calculates Your Benefit

The SSA uses a formula called **AIME (Average Indexed Monthly Earnings)** that works like this:

1. **Collect your annual earnings** for every year you had Social Security-taxable income
2. **Index those earnings for wage inflation** using the SSA's national average wage index (so a dollar earned in 1975 gets adjusted upward)
3. **Select your highest 35 years** of indexed earnings
4. **Average those 35 years** and divide by 12 to get your monthly AIME
5. **Apply bend points** to calculate your Primary Insurance Amount (PIA)

The 2025 PIA formula applies three rates to your AIME:

| AIME Range | Replacement Rate |
|---|---|
| First $1,174 | 90% |
| $1,174 to $7,078 | 32% |
| Above $7,078 | 15% |

This progressive formula means lower-income workers replace a higher percentage of their earnings than higher-income workers.

## Why Teenage Work Years Often Don't Count

There are a few reasons early work years may not factor into your benefit:

- **Earnings below the reporting threshold.** Before 1978, employers only reported annual totals to the SSA if you earned above a minimum threshold. Cash-paid teenage jobs at restaurants or farms often went unreported.
- **No Social Security number on file.** Some teens worked before obtaining a Social Security number, especially before the Tax Reform Act of 1986 required SSNs for dependents claimed on tax returns.
- **35-year averaging.** Even if those teenage earnings were reported, they'd only enter the calculation if they ranked among your **highest 35 years** of indexed earnings. A few hundred dollars earned at 13 in 1969, even indexed for inflation, is likely lower than what you earned in your 30s, 40s, or 50s.
- **Pre-1951 records.** The SSA's electronic records system starts in 1951. Earnings before that date require manual verification through old tax records, which the IRS destroys after 6-7 years.

For most people who worked consistently through their 20s-60s, teenage earnings get pushed out of the top 35 anyway.

## How to Check Your Own Earnings Record

The SSA provides a free tool at **ssa.gov/myaccount** where you can:

1. View your complete earnings history year by year
2. See estimated monthly benefits at ages 62, 67, and 70
3. Identify any years with missing or incorrect earnings
4. File a correction request if you have W-2s or tax returns as proof

According to the SSA, approximately **1 in 20** earnings records contains at least one error. The SSA allows corrections if you can provide documentation, but the further back you go, the harder proof is to find.

## The Bigger Picture: What Your Benefit Actually Provides

Even with a perfect earnings record, Social Security was designed to replace only a portion of pre-retirement income. Here's what the SSA's own data shows:

| Earnings Level | Replacement Rate |
|---|---|
| Low earners (~$28,000/year) | About 56% |
| Medium earners (~$63,000/year) | About 41% |
| High earners (~$131,000/year) | About 29% |
| Maximum earners ($168,600+ in 2024) | About 25% |

Source: SSA Office of the Chief Actuary, 2024 estimates.

The average retired worker received **$1,907/month** as of January 2025. The maximum benefit at full retirement age (67 for people born in 1960 or later) was **$3,822/month**. Even at the maximum, that's $45,864 per year.

The Bureau of Labor Statistics reports average annual expenditures for households aged 65-74 at **$60,087** in 2023. Social Security, by design, doesn't cover the full bill.

## What Working Longer Actually Does

For the MarketWatch reader's specific question about working into his late 60s, here's what the SSA says:

- **If you have fewer than 35 years of earnings,** additional work years replace $0 years in the formula, which can increase your benefit.
- **If you already have 35+ years,** new earnings only help if they're higher than your lowest year in the top 35 (after indexing).
- **Delayed retirement credits** increase benefits by 8% per year for each year you delay claiming past full retirement age, up to age 70.

A 69-year-old who claimed at 67 already maxed out the full retirement age benefit. Part-time work at 69 would only raise the benefit if those earnings exceeded one of the existing 35 years in the formula.

## Building Income Beyond Social Security

The Federal Reserve's 2022 Survey of Consumer Finances found that retirees with **three or more income sources** (Social Security, retirement accounts, investment income, part-time work) had significantly higher financial security than those dependent on one or two.

Options for building additional retirement income include:

- **Max out IRA catch-up contributions:** $8,000 total for those 50+ in 2025
- **Consider Roth conversions** during lower-income years to create tax-free future income
- **Diversify asset types:** The World Gold Council reports gold returned 8.4% annualized over 20 years ending December 2024, with low correlation to stocks (approximately 0.05 over 30 years)
- **Use a self-directed IRA** to hold physical gold, silver, real estate, or other assets not available in standard brokerage accounts

The SSA itself states that Social Security was designed as one part of retirement income, not the whole thing. Building the other parts is up to you.
