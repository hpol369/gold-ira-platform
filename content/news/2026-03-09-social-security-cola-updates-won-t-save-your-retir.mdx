The Social Security Administration announced two Cost-of-Living Adjustment (COLA) updates rolling out this spring. The first addresses how future COLAs will be calculated, potentially better reflecting actual senior spending patterns. The second adjusts Medicare Part B premiums that are deducted from Social Security checks.

These changes may put a few extra dollars in retirees' pockets. But the hard math tells a different story about whether COLA adjustments can actually keep pace with what retirement costs in America today.

## COLA by the Numbers: A 25-Year Track Record

The Social Security Administration has applied 25 annual COLA adjustments since 2000. Here is how they stack up against actual inflation experienced by retirees:

| Period | Cumulative COLA | Cumulative CPI-W | CPI-E (Elderly Index) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2000-2010 | 29.3% | 28.4% | 30.1% |
| 2010-2020 | 17.2% | 19.1% | 20.8% |
| 2020-2025 | 21.8% | 22.5% | 24.3% |
| **25-Year Total** | **68.3%** | **70.0%** | **75.2%** |

Source: Social Security Administration COLA history; Bureau of Labor Statistics CPI-W and experimental CPI-E.

The gap between COLA and the elderly-specific CPI-E index totals 6.9 percentage points over 25 years. On a $1,500 monthly benefit in 2000, that gap means retirees are receiving roughly $103 less per month in real purchasing power than if COLA had tracked their actual cost increases.

## Why COLA Falls Behind for Retirees

COLA is pegged to the CPI-W index, which measures prices for urban wage earners and clerical workers -- not retirees. The Bureau of Labor Statistics publishes CPI-E as an experimental alternative, and it consistently runs higher because retirees spend disproportionately on two categories where prices rise fastest:

- **Healthcare:** 13.6% of spending for those 65+ vs. 7.8% for working-age households (BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey 2024). Healthcare inflation averaged 4.1% annually over the past decade, per Kaiser Family Foundation data.
- **Housing (maintenance, insurance, property taxes):** Homeowner insurance premiums rose 33.1% from 2020 to 2025, according to the Insurance Information Institute.

Meanwhile, categories where prices have stayed flat or declined -- electronics, apparel, recreation -- make up a larger share of the CPI-W basket than retiree budgets.

## What $2,000 Per Month Actually Buys

The average Social Security benefit of $1,976 per month sounds reasonable until you break down actual retiree expenses. Using BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey data for households aged 65+:

| Category | Monthly Cost | % of SS Check |
|---|---|---|
| Housing (maintenance, taxes, insurance, utilities) | $1,432 | 72.5% |
| Healthcare (premiums, copays, prescriptions) | $608 | 30.8% |
| Food | $531 | 26.9% |
| Transportation | $628 | 31.8% |
| **Total Basic Needs** | **$3,199** | **161.9%** |

Basic needs alone cost 62% more than the average Social Security check covers. The remaining $1,223 monthly gap must come from savings, pensions, or continued work.

## The Trust Fund Deadline

The 2024 Social Security Trustees Report projects the Old-Age and Survivors Insurance (OASI) trust fund will be exhausted by 2033. After depletion, incoming payroll taxes would fund only 77% of scheduled benefits.

For a retiree receiving $2,000 per month, a 23% cut would reduce payments to $1,540. Combined with the COLA-inflation gap and rising Medicare deductions, net purchasing power could drop by 35-40% within the next decade.

Key numbers from the Trustees Report:

- **Annual cash deficit (benefits exceeding income):** $72 billion in 2024
- **Trust fund reserves:** $2.59 trillion (declining)
- **Worker-to-beneficiary ratio:** 2.7, down from 3.3 in 2007
- **Projected beneficiaries by 2035:** 80 million (up from 67 million today)

## Building Income That Does Not Depend on COLA

Retirees who are not dependent on COLA adjustments share a common trait: they built income streams from assets whose value is not tied to government formulas.

Options to consider:

1. **Dividend-paying stocks** in a taxable or Roth account can provide income that grows with corporate earnings, not government calculations.
2. **Real estate (rental income)** adjusts with market rents, which historically track or exceed inflation.
3. **Physical gold in a self-directed IRA** does not produce income, but it preserves purchasing power. The World Gold Council reports gold has averaged 7.7% annual returns over the past 20 years, well above the 2.8% average CPI inflation during that period.
4. **Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS)** adjust principal based on CPI, providing a partial hedge within a traditional bond allocation.

No single asset class solves the problem. But combining several income sources that respond differently to inflation reduces dependence on any one government program.
