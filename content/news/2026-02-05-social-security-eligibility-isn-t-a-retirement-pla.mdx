According to the Employee Benefit Research Institute, 28% of retirees cite Social Security eligibility as a primary reason they stopped working. Nearly one in three Americans are using a government payment schedule as their retirement plan — and the math shows why that is a problem.

Social Security was designed in 1935 as a supplement to personal savings, not a replacement for them. Understanding the actual numbers behind the program helps explain why relying on it alone leaves most retirees short.

## The Real Numbers Behind Social Security

The Social Security Administration reports these figures for 2025:

- **Average retirement benefit:** $1,976 per month ($23,712 per year)
- **Maximum benefit at age 62:** $2,831 per month
- **Maximum benefit at age 70:** $5,108 per month
- **Cost-of-living adjustment (COLA) for 2025:** 2.5%

That COLA is supposed to keep pace with inflation. But the Bureau of Labor Statistics data tells a different story. Between 2020 and 2024, cumulative CPI inflation was 23.2%, while cumulative Social Security COLAs totaled approximately 21.1%. Retirees lost ground — roughly 2 percentage points of purchasing power in just four years.

**Over a 20-year retirement, even a small annual gap between inflation and COLA compounds into a serious shortfall.** A benefit worth $1,976 today will buy the equivalent of roughly $1,340 in goods and services 20 years from now if inflation averages just 2% more than COLAs annually.

## The Trust Fund Timeline

The Social Security Board of Trustees 2024 Annual Report projects that the Old-Age and Survivors Insurance (OASI) trust fund will be depleted by 2033. After that date, incoming payroll taxes would cover only about 79% of scheduled benefits.

That does not mean Social Security disappears. It means one of three things happens:

1. **Benefits get cut by approximately 21%** (the automatic outcome if Congress does nothing)
2. **Payroll taxes increase** (currently 6.2% on earnings up to $176,100 in 2025)
3. **The retirement age increases** beyond the current 67 for those born after 1960

None of those outcomes help someone who built their retirement plan around a full Social Security check.

## What a Social-Security-Dependent Retirement Looks Like

A retired construction worker claiming at 62 today receives roughly $1,550 per month (reduced for early filing). After Medicare Part B premiums ($185/month in 2025), take-home drops to about $1,365 per month.

| Monthly Expense | Estimated Cost |
|---|---|
| Housing (modest mortgage or rent) | $600-$900 |
| Utilities | $150-$250 |
| Groceries (one person) | $350-$450 |
| Medicare Part B + supplement | $185 + $150 |
| Transportation | $200-$350 |
| **Total** | **$1,635 - $2,285** |

Even at the low end, $1,365 per month does not cover basic expenses. The gap ranges from $270 to $920 per month. That is $3,240 to $11,040 per year that has to come from somewhere.

This is why the SSA itself states that Social Security replaces only about 40% of pre-retirement income for average earners — and less for higher earners.

## Building Beyond Social Security

The retirees who avoid this squeeze have income from multiple sources. According to the Federal Reserve's 2022 Survey of Consumer Finances, households in the top quartile of retirement preparedness typically hold:

- **Employer retirement plans** (401(k), 403(b), pension)
- **Personal IRAs** (traditional and Roth)
- **Real assets** (real estate, precious metals)
- **Taxable investment accounts**

The key is diversification across asset types, not just across stocks and bonds. Holding everything in paper assets means your entire retirement is tied to the same financial system — the stock market, the dollar, and government monetary policy.

## Where Precious Metals Fit

Gold has served as a store of value across every major inflationary period in modern U.S. history. During the high-inflation decade of 1972-1982 (when CPI averaged 8.7% annually), gold rose from $64 per ounce to $456 per ounce — a 612% increase that far outpaced inflation.

A self-directed IRA lets you roll over existing 401(k) or traditional IRA funds into physical gold and silver with no taxes and no penalties. The IRS allows gold bullion meeting .995 fineness standards (American Eagles, Canadian Maple Leafs, and others) to be held inside an IRA under IRC Section 408(m)(3).

**This is not about replacing your entire portfolio with gold.** A 5-15% allocation to precious metals inside your retirement account provides a hedge that moves independently of stocks and bonds. When the S&P 500 dropped 19.4% in 2022, gold finished the year essentially flat — doing exactly what a diversifier is supposed to do.

## The Actionable Takeaway

Do not let your Social Security eligibility date drive your retirement decision. Instead, calculate what you actually need:

1. **Add up your monthly expenses** (be honest — include healthcare, car repairs, home maintenance)
2. **Check your projected Social Security benefit** at ssa.gov/myaccount
3. **Calculate the gap** between those two numbers
4. **Build a plan to fill that gap** from 401(k) withdrawals, IRA income, and diversified assets

If your retirement savings are concentrated in one asset class — especially if it is all in an employer 401(k) you have not reviewed in years — a rollover into a self-directed IRA gives you the flexibility to diversify into assets that do not all move in the same direction at the same time.
