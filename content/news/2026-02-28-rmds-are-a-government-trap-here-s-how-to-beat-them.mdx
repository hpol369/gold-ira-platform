Starting at age 73 (raised from 72 by the SECURE 2.0 Act of 2022, and scheduled to rise to 75 in 2033), the IRS requires you to withdraw a minimum amount from your traditional IRAs and 401(k)s every year. These Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) are calculated by dividing your account balance by a life expectancy factor from the IRS Uniform Lifetime Table.

Yahoo Finance recently ran a piece on strategies to manage RMDs, and the topic deserves a closer look -- because the tax consequences extend well beyond the obvious.

## How RMDs Are Calculated

The IRS uses the Uniform Lifetime Table (updated in 2022) to determine your distribution factor. Here is what the actual numbers look like at various ages:

| Age | Distribution Factor | RMD on $500,000 | RMD on $750,000 | RMD on $1,000,000 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 73 | 26.5 | $18,868 | $28,302 | $37,736 |
| 75 | 24.6 | $20,325 | $30,488 | $40,650 |
| 78 | 22.0 | $22,727 | $34,091 | $45,455 |
| 80 | 20.2 | $24,752 | $37,129 | $49,505 |
| 85 | 16.0 | $31,250 | $46,875 | $62,500 |

*Source: IRS Publication 590-B, Uniform Lifetime Table (2022 revision)*

The penalty for missing an RMD was reduced from 50% to 25% by SECURE 2.0 (and to 10% if corrected within two years), but that is still a steep price for a paperwork mistake.

## The Hidden Tax Cascade

The problem with RMDs is not just the distribution itself. It is the chain reaction that forced withdrawals trigger across multiple tax provisions:

**1. Income tax bracket creep.** A $20,000 RMD added to $55,000 in Social Security and pension income pushes a single filer from the 22% bracket into the 24% bracket (2024 thresholds: $100,525 for the 24% bracket).

**2. Social Security taxation.** Once combined income exceeds $34,000 (single) or $44,000 (married filing jointly), up to 85% of Social Security benefits become taxable. An RMD can push you over that threshold. According to the Social Security Administration, 56% of beneficiaries now pay taxes on their benefits -- up from 10% when the thresholds were set in 1984.

**3. Medicare IRMAA surcharges.** If your modified adjusted gross income exceeds $103,000 (single) or $206,000 (married), you pay higher Medicare Part B and Part D premiums. The surcharges in 2025:

| Income Level (Single) | Part B Monthly Premium | Part D Monthly Surcharge |
|---|---|---|
| $103,000 or less | $185.00 | $0.00 |
| $103,001 - $129,000 | $259.00 | $13.70 |
| $129,001 - $161,000 | $370.00 | $35.30 |
| $161,001 - $193,000 | $480.90 | $57.00 |
| $193,001 - $500,000 | $591.90 | $78.60 |

*Source: CMS Medicare Premiums 2025*

A single RMD that pushes you from the $103,000 bracket to the $129,000 bracket costs an extra $888 per year in Part B premiums alone -- on top of the income tax on the RMD itself.

**4. Net Investment Income Tax.** High earners may also trigger the 3.8% NIIT on investment income when RMDs push their modified AGI above $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married).

## Five Legitimate Strategies to Reduce RMD Impact

**Strategy 1: Roth conversions before age 73.** Converting traditional IRA funds to a Roth during lower-income years (such as the gap between retirement and age 73) means those funds are no longer subject to RMDs. Roth IRAs have no RMD requirement during the owner's lifetime.

**Strategy 2: Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs).** If you are 70.5 or older, you can donate up to $105,000 per year (2024 limit, indexed for inflation) directly from your IRA to a qualified charity. The QCD counts toward your RMD but is not included in taxable income. This is one of the most efficient tax strategies available to retirees who already give to charity.

**Strategy 3: Still working at 73?** If you are still employed and your company offers a 401(k), you can delay RMDs from that specific plan until you retire. This does not apply to IRAs or 401(k)s from previous employers.

**Strategy 4: Diversify into Roth accounts now.** If your employer offers a Roth 401(k) option, future contributions grow tax-free and are not subject to RMDs (after rollover to a Roth IRA). The SECURE 2.0 Act also eliminated RMDs for employer Roth 401(k) accounts starting in 2024.

**Strategy 5: Hold physical assets in a self-directed IRA.** While self-directed IRAs holding gold or silver are still subject to RMD rules, they offer a key advantage: you control the timing and method of liquidation more precisely. You can take your RMD in-kind (receiving physical gold rather than cash), and the tax is based on the fair market value at the time of distribution. This lets you sell on your schedule rather than being forced to liquidate stocks at a bad time.

## Important Clarification on Gold IRAs and RMDs

To be straightforward: a self-directed IRA holding physical gold is still subject to RMD requirements. There is no way to completely avoid RMDs on traditional IRA assets, regardless of what those assets are.

However, there are practical advantages:

- Gold held in an IRA can be distributed in-kind -- you receive the physical metal rather than being forced to sell at a potentially bad time
- Gold's historical performance during inflationary periods (up 67% cumulatively from 2020-2024, per the World Gold Council) means the asset itself may better preserve value compared to bonds or cash
- Diversifying a portion of your IRA into gold reduces your concentration in assets that all react the same way to market downturns

## The Bottom Line

RMDs are a fact of life for anyone with traditional retirement accounts. You cannot avoid them, but you can plan around them. The most effective approach combines:

1. Roth conversions during lower-income years
2. QCDs if you give to charity
3. Diversification across asset types so you are not forced to sell a single asset class at the worst possible time
