More employers are nudging workers -- especially younger ones -- into "managed account" 401(k) plans that use algorithms and professional managers to automatically adjust portfolios. MarketWatch reports these plans are growing rapidly, with proponents claiming they could boost retirement outcomes by up to 22% compared to workers who pick their own investments.

The pitch sounds appealing: let professionals handle the decisions, and you end up with more money. But the details tell a more complicated story, especially for workers over 55 who are closest to needing that money.

## How Managed 401(k) Accounts Work

Traditional 401(k) plans give you a menu of funds -- typically 15-30 options -- and you pick your own allocation. Managed accounts replace that choice with an algorithm or human advisor who sets your allocation based on your age, salary, and expected retirement date.

Here is how the fee structures compare:

| Plan Type | Typical Annual Fee | Fee on $500,000 Balance | 20-Year Fee Total |
|---|---|---|---|
| Index fund 401(k) | 0.03-0.10% | $150-$500 | $3,000-$10,000 |
| Target-date fund | 0.10-0.30% | $500-$1,500 | $10,000-$30,000 |
| Managed account | 0.40-1.00% | $2,000-$5,000 | $40,000-$100,000 |

*Sources: Morningstar 401(k) Fee Study 2024, Investment Company Institute*

That fee difference is significant. On a $500,000 balance, the jump from a 0.05% index fund to a 0.70% managed account costs roughly $3,250 per year. Over 10 years, that is $32,500 in extra fees -- money that comes directly out of your retirement balance.

## Does Active Management Actually Deliver?

The 22% improvement figure deserves scrutiny. According to a 2023 Alight Solutions study, the comparison is between managed accounts and the worst do-it-yourself investors -- people who kept 100% in a money market fund or loaded up entirely on company stock.

When you compare managed accounts to simple target-date funds or a basic three-fund portfolio (total stock market, international stock, total bond), the picture changes:

- **S&P Dow Jones SPIVA Scorecard (2024):** 88% of actively managed large-cap funds underperformed the S&P 500 over 15 years
- **Morningstar Active/Passive Barometer (2024):** Only 27% of active funds beat their passive benchmarks over a 10-year period
- **Vanguard Research (2023):** Investors in simple target-date funds achieved 90% of the returns of managed accounts, at roughly one-third the cost

The data consistently shows that a basic, low-cost index fund approach gets you most of the return at a fraction of the fee.

## The Real Limitation: Asset Class Restrictions

Whether you choose a managed account or pick your own funds, your 401(k) limits you to the same asset classes:

- U.S. stocks
- International stocks
- Bonds (government and corporate)
- Money market funds
- Possibly REITs or commodities funds (rare)

What you cannot hold in a standard 401(k):

- Physical gold or silver
- Individual real estate
- Private business interests
- Direct commodity ownership

This matters because true diversification means owning assets that behave differently from each other. During the 2022 inflation shock, the typical 60/40 stock-bond portfolio fell 17% (Morningstar). Stocks and bonds dropped together for the first time since 1969. A managed account holding the same two asset classes would have performed no differently.

Meanwhile, physical gold finished 2022 down just 0.3% (World Gold Council). It was essentially flat while both legs of the standard portfolio fell.

## What to Consider Before Switching

**If your employer offers a managed account option, ask these questions:**

1. What is the total annual fee, including underlying fund expenses?
2. What asset classes does the managed account invest in? (If it is just stocks and bonds in different proportions, you can replicate that with a target-date fund for less.)
3. Can you split your contributions -- some to the managed account, some to a self-directed option?
4. What is your rollover eligibility? If you are 55 or older, many plans allow in-service rollovers to IRAs while still employed.

**For workers approaching retirement, the rollover question is critical.** An in-service rollover lets you move a portion of your 401(k) to a self-directed IRA where you can invest in physical gold, silver, real estate, and other assets not available inside a 401(k). You keep contributing to the 401(k) for the employer match while diversifying the rest.

According to the IRS, rollovers from 401(k) to IRA are tax-free and penalty-free when done as a direct trustee-to-trustee transfer.

## The Broader Point

A managed 401(k) account is not inherently bad. For someone who would otherwise keep everything in cash or make emotional trades, it probably helps. But it is not innovation -- it is paying more for the same asset classes with a different label.

Real diversification means owning things that do not all move in the same direction at the same time. Physical precious metals, held in a self-directed IRA, provide that diversification in a way that no combination of stock and bond funds can match.
