A 64-year-old reader wrote to MarketWatch with a situation that millions of Americans can relate to: zero retirement savings, $900 per month in Social Security benefits, and a $400,000 inheritance they do not know how to handle. After decades of working, their entire financial future depends on decisions made in the next 12 months.

This is not a rare case. The Federal Reserve's Survey of Consumer Finances shows 25% of households aged 55-64 have less than $10,000 in retirement savings. The National Institute on Retirement Security estimates 40% of all U.S. households will be unable to maintain their standard of living in retirement.

## The $400,000 Math Problem

At first glance, $400,000 sounds substantial. But the numbers tell a different story when you run them against actual retirement costs.

**Current income without inheritance:**
- Social Security: $900/month ($10,800/year)
- The 2025 federal poverty line for a single person: $15,650/year
- Monthly shortfall below poverty: $404

**Income scenarios with $400,000 invested:**

| Strategy | Annual Income Generated | Combined with SS | Monthly Total |
|---|---|---|---|
| Savings account (4.5% APY) | $18,000 | $28,800 | $2,400 |
| 60/40 stock/bond portfolio (4% withdrawal) | $16,000 | $26,800 | $2,233 |
| Bond ladder (5% yield) | $20,000 | $30,800 | $2,567 |
| Annuity (age 65, single life) | $26,400 | $37,200 | $3,100 |

Source: Current CD/savings rates from Bankrate (March 2026); annuity estimates from ImmediateAnnuities.com.

The best-case scenario -- an immediate annuity -- generates $3,100 per month. The Bureau of Labor Statistics Consumer Expenditure Survey shows the average household aged 65+ spends $4,818 per month. Even with the inheritance, there is a $1,718 monthly gap.

## Why $400,000 Does Not Go as Far as It Used To

The purchasing power of $400,000 has dropped significantly. According to BLS CPI data:

- **$400,000 in 2020 purchasing power** is worth approximately $311,000 in 2026 dollars
- **At 3% annual inflation**, $400,000 will buy what $295,000 buys in 10 years and $219,000 in 20 years
- **Healthcare inflation** runs even faster: Fidelity estimates a single 65-year-old male needs $165,000 and a female needs $197,000 for lifetime healthcare costs

A 64-year-old today has a life expectancy of approximately 84 for men and 87 for women, per the Social Security Administration Actuarial Life Table. That means the $400,000 needs to last 20-23 years.

At a 4% withdrawal rate ($16,000/year), the money runs out in roughly 25 years if invested conservatively. But that assumes no major healthcare expenses, no market crashes during early retirement, and inflation stays moderate.

## The Sequence-of-Returns Problem

This person faces an acute version of "sequence-of-returns risk." If they invest the $400,000 and the market drops 25% in year one, they are left with $300,000 -- and still need to withdraw $16,000 for living expenses. That $284,000 now needs to recover 41% just to get back to the original withdrawal capacity.

The Center for Retirement Research at Boston College found that retirees who experience a 20%+ market loss in their first two years of retirement have a 57% higher probability of outliving their savings compared to those who start during flat or up markets.

For someone with no other savings to fall back on, one bad year could permanently damage their financial security.

## The Social Security Timing Decision

At 64, this person has already claimed Social Security early, locking in a reduced benefit. According to SSA data:

- **Claiming at 62** (likely their case) reduces benefits by 30% compared to full retirement age
- **Full retirement age benefit** (if they had waited to 67): Approximately $1,286/month
- **Delayed to 70:** Approximately $1,594/month
- **Income permanently lost by claiming early:** $386-$694/month for life

This illustrates why financial planning needs to happen years before retirement, not after an inheritance lands.

## A Practical Framework for a Late-Start Windfall

For someone in this position, financial planners generally recommend a tiered approach:

1. **Emergency fund (6 months expenses):** $15,000 in a high-yield savings account. Non-negotiable. This prevents forced selling during downturns.

2. **Healthcare reserve:** Set aside $40,000-$50,000 specifically for medical expenses, dental work, and potential long-term care gaps before Medicare kicks in at 65.

3. **Income floor:** Consider using $100,000-$150,000 to purchase a single-premium immediate annuity, creating guaranteed monthly income that supplements Social Security.

4. **Growth and inflation protection:** Invest the remaining $185,000-$245,000 across asset classes designed to preserve purchasing power:
   - Broad stock market index funds for growth
   - Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS) for inflation hedging
   - Physical gold in a self-directed IRA for crisis protection and long-term purchasing-power preservation

The World Gold Council reports gold has outpaced inflation in 75% of 5-year rolling periods since 1971, making it a reliable long-term hedge for retirees concerned about the dollar's declining value.

## The Bigger Lesson

This person's situation is a warning for anyone over 50 who has not diversified their retirement strategy. The Federal Reserve data shows the median retirement account balance for households aged 55-64 is just $185,000. For many, an inheritance or home equity is the largest asset they will ever hold.

The key is not trying to hit home runs with that money. It is building a structure that generates reliable income while protecting against the two biggest retirement threats: market crashes and inflation.
