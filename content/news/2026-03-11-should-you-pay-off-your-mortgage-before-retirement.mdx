With 30-year mortgage rates settling around 5.75% as of March 2026 (Freddie Mac Primary Mortgage Market Survey), the question of whether to carry a mortgage into retirement has gotten more complicated. At 2-3% rates in 2021, the math strongly favored keeping the mortgage. At today's rates, the answer depends on your specific numbers.

Here is how to think through the decision using actual data rather than blanket advice.

## The Current Rate Environment

According to Freddie Mac, the average 30-year fixed mortgage rate has moved through these phases:

| Year | Average 30-Year Rate |
|---|---|
| 2021 | 2.96% |
| 2022 | 5.34% |
| 2023 | 6.81% |
| 2024 | 6.72% |
| 2025 | 6.08% |
| 2026 (YTD) | 5.75% |

If you locked in during 2020-2021, your rate is likely between 2.5% and 3.5%. If you refinanced or purchased more recently, you are probably paying 5.5% to 7%.

The decision framework is different for each group.

## The Math: Pay Off vs. Keep the Mortgage

Consider two scenarios for someone with $200,000 remaining on their mortgage and $200,000 in savings.

**Scenario A: Pay off the mortgage**
- Eliminates $200,000 in debt
- Saves interest payments (varies by rate)
- Leaves $0 in liquid savings
- No flexibility for emergencies or opportunities

**Scenario B: Keep the mortgage, invest the savings**

| Your Mortgage Rate | Annual Interest Cost | After Tax Deduction (24% bracket) | Break-Even Investment Return Needed |
|---|---|---|---|
| 3.0% | $6,000 | $4,560 | 2.3% |
| 4.5% | $9,000 | $6,840 | 3.4% |
| 5.5% | $11,000 | $8,360 | 4.2% |
| 6.5% | $13,000 | $9,880 | 4.9% |

At a 3% mortgage, you only need a 2.3% after-tax return to come out ahead by keeping the mortgage. Treasury bills currently yield around 4.5%, so the math favors keeping the debt.

At 6.5%, you need a 4.9% after-tax return to break even -- tighter, but still achievable with a diversified portfolio.

## What the Federal Reserve Data Shows About Retiree Debt

The Federal Reserve's Survey of Consumer Finances (2022) reveals:

- **38% of households aged 65-74** carry mortgage debt
- **Median mortgage balance for 65-74:** $110,000
- **23% of households aged 75+** still carry a mortgage
- **Median mortgage balance for 75+:** $75,000

These numbers have increased significantly. In 1989, only 18% of households aged 65-74 had mortgage debt, per the same Fed survey. The shift reflects higher home prices, later home purchases, and cash-out refinancing during the low-rate era.

## The Liquidity Question

The biggest risk of paying off the mortgage is liquidity. The Federal Reserve reports that the median checking and savings balance for households aged 65-74 is $54,100. After paying off a $110,000 mortgage from savings, many retirees would have almost nothing in accessible cash.

Why this matters:

- **Healthcare emergencies.** Fidelity estimates a 65-year-old couple needs $365,000 for lifetime healthcare costs. Out-of-pocket expenses can spike unpredictably.
- **Home repairs.** The Joint Center for Housing Studies at Harvard reports that homeowners 65+ spend an average of $8,200 per year on maintenance and repairs.
- **Market downturns.** If your remaining retirement funds are in a 401(k) and stocks drop 25%, you may be forced to sell at the worst possible time to cover basic expenses.

Keeping the mortgage and maintaining a liquid reserve of 18-24 months of expenses provides a buffer against all three risks.

## The Inflation Angle

One factor working in favor of keeping a fixed-rate mortgage: your payment stays the same while inflation pushes up the cost of everything else. The Bureau of Labor Statistics reports cumulative inflation of 22.5% from 2020 to 2025. A $1,200 monthly mortgage payment in 2020 dollars is effectively $978 in today's purchasing power.

Over a 20-year retirement, that erosion continues. At 3% annual inflation, a $1,200 payment in 2026 would feel like a $664 payment in 2046 dollars.

Meanwhile, assets that track or exceed inflation -- including real estate equity, dividend stocks, and gold -- tend to grow in nominal terms while the fixed mortgage payment shrinks in real terms. The World Gold Council reports gold's annualized return of 7.7% over the past 20 years has comfortably outpaced the 2.8% average CPI inflation during that period.

## A Practical Decision Framework

Answer these three questions:

1. **What is your mortgage rate?** Below 4.5%: strong case for keeping it. Above 6.5%: strong case for paying it off. Between 4.5-6.5%: depends on your other assets and risk tolerance.

2. **How much liquid savings do you have beyond the mortgage balance?** If paying off the mortgage leaves you with less than 18 months of expenses in cash, keep the mortgage.

3. **What is your total income in retirement?** If your mortgage payment exceeds 25% of your combined Social Security, pension, and portfolio income, reducing or eliminating it may reduce monthly stress.

There is no universal right answer. But the data consistently shows that maintaining liquidity and diversification in retirement is more important than the psychological comfort of a paid-off house.
