New data from Yahoo Finance reveals that even mortgage-free retirees cannot cover basic living expenses on Social Security alone in at least 10 states. That includes Hawaii, Massachusetts, California, New York, and several others where costs simply outrun the average benefit check.

The Social Security Administration reports the average retired worker benefit at $1,976 per month as of January 2025. In the states flagged by this analysis, basic expenses -- food, utilities, healthcare, transportation, and insurance -- exceed that amount even without a housing payment.

## Where Social Security Falls Short

Here is how monthly costs stack up against average benefits in the most expensive states, based on Bureau of Labor Statistics and Missouri Economic Research and Information Center (MERIC) cost-of-living data:

| State | Avg Monthly Expenses (No Mortgage) | Avg SS Benefit | Monthly Shortfall |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hawaii | $2,890 | $1,976 | -$914 |
| Massachusetts | $2,640 | $1,976 | -$664 |
| California | $2,580 | $1,976 | -$604 |
| New York | $2,520 | $1,976 | -$544 |
| Alaska | $2,470 | $1,976 | -$494 |
| Connecticut | $2,410 | $1,976 | -$434 |

Even in cheaper states like Mississippi and West Virginia, the margin is razor-thin -- sometimes under $200 per month of breathing room.

## The Trust Fund Reality

The Social Security Board of Trustees projects the Old-Age and Survivors Insurance (OASI) trust fund will be depleted by 2033. After that, incoming payroll taxes would cover only about 77% of scheduled benefits, according to the 2024 Trustees Report.

That does not mean Social Security disappears. But it does mean potential benefit cuts of roughly 23% unless Congress acts. For someone receiving $1,976 monthly, a 23% cut would reduce payments to about $1,522 per month.

Key trust fund numbers:

- **Current worker-to-beneficiary ratio:** 2.7 workers per beneficiary (down from 3.3 in 2007), per SSA data
- **Annual shortfall starting in 2033:** Approximately $300 billion per year
- **Total unfunded obligation over 75 years:** $22.4 trillion

## Why COLA Adjustments Have Not Kept Pace

Social Security Cost-of-Living Adjustments (COLA) are tied to the Consumer Price Index for Urban Wage Earners (CPI-W). The problem: CPI-W tracks spending patterns of working-age urban households, not retirees.

The Bureau of Labor Statistics does publish an experimental index for the elderly -- CPI-E -- which consistently runs 0.2 to 0.3 percentage points higher than CPI-W annually. Over a 25-year retirement, that gap compounds to roughly 5-8% in lost purchasing power.

Specific categories where retirees spend more than working-age adults:

- **Healthcare:** 13.6% of retiree budgets vs. 7.8% for workers (BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey)
- **Housing maintenance:** Older homeowners face rising repair costs on aging properties
- **Prescription drugs:** Out-of-pocket drug spending for Medicare beneficiaries averaged $1,536 per year in 2023, per the Kaiser Family Foundation

## Filling the Gap: What the Numbers Say

If Social Security covers roughly 40% of pre-retirement income (the SSA's own estimate for average earners), retirees need other income sources for the remaining 60%.

Here is what it takes to generate $1,100 per month ($13,200 per year) from savings at various withdrawal rates:

| Withdrawal Rate | Savings Needed |
|---|---|
| 4% (traditional rule) | $330,000 |
| 3.5% (more conservative) | $377,000 |
| 3% (for 30+ year retirement) | $440,000 |

According to the Federal Reserve's Survey of Consumer Finances, the median retirement account balance for households aged 55-64 is $185,000. That generates only $617 per month at a 4% withdrawal rate -- far short of the gap.

## Diversification Beyond Dollar-Denominated Assets

When both your Social Security check and your savings account are denominated in the same currency, inflation hits twice. Holding a portion of retirement assets in hard assets like gold introduces a different risk profile.

The World Gold Council reports gold has averaged 7.7% annual returns over the past 20 years, outpacing CPI inflation of 2.8% over the same period. During the highest-inflation years of 2021-2023, gold returned 35.7% cumulatively while the S&P 500 returned 21.4%.
