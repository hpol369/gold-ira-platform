A MarketWatch reader recently posed a question that cuts to the heart of modern retirement planning. This couple has $2.5 million in savings, contributes $7,000 per month to taxable accounts, and maxes out both 401(k)s. Their concern: almost nothing sits in Roth IRAs. Should they pursue a mega backdoor Roth conversion?

It is a valid question. But it also reveals a blind spot in how most Americans -- even wealthy ones -- think about retirement security.

## What a Mega Backdoor Roth Actually Is

For those unfamiliar, a mega backdoor Roth is a strategy that allows high earners to funnel after-tax 401(k) contributions into a Roth IRA, bypassing the normal income limits. Here is how the contribution landscape looks in 2025:

| Account Type | 2025 Limit | Tax Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Traditional/Roth IRA | $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+) | Pre-tax or post-tax |
| 401(k) employee contribution | $23,500 ($31,000 if 50+) | Pre-tax or Roth |
| Total 401(k) limit (incl. employer) | $70,000 ($77,500 if 50+) | Mixed |
| Mega backdoor Roth (after-tax portion) | Up to $46,500 | After-tax, converted to Roth |

*Source: IRS Revenue Procedure 2024-40*

The strategy works and is legal. But it is also complex, requires a 401(k) plan that allows after-tax contributions and in-plan conversions, and it keeps 100% of your wealth inside the traditional financial system.

## The Concentration Problem Nobody Discusses

This couple's $2.5 million is almost certainly allocated across some combination of stocks, bonds, and mutual funds. That is standard financial planning advice. But consider what that means:

- **100% dollar-denominated.** Every dollar they own is subject to purchasing power erosion from inflation and currency debasement
- **100% dependent on market performance.** The S&P 500 has had twelve bear markets since 1950, with an average decline of 33.5% (Hartford Funds data)
- **100% inside the tax system.** Traditional IRA and 401(k) withdrawals are taxed as ordinary income. RMDs begin at 73 and force taxable distributions whether you need the money or not
- **100% subject to rule changes.** Congress has modified retirement account rules multiple times -- SECURE Act (2019), SECURE 2.0 (2022), and the proposed changes in various budget bills

The 2022 Inflation Reduction Act and SECURE 2.0 both contained provisions that affected retirement accounts. The Build Back Better bill that nearly passed in 2021 would have prohibited mega backdoor Roth conversions entirely and capped Roth account balances at $10 million. That specific provision failed, but it signals where policy may be heading.

## What High-Net-Worth Investors Actually Do

According to the Knight Frank Wealth Report (2024), ultra-high-net-worth individuals allocate their assets very differently from the typical 401(k) investor:

| Asset Class | Average UHNW Allocation | Typical 401(k) Allocation |
|---|---|---|
| Stocks/equities | 33% | 60-70% |
| Real estate | 27% | 0% |
| Bonds/fixed income | 17% | 20-30% |
| Private equity/business | 10% | 0% |
| Gold/precious metals | 5% | 0% |
| Cash | 5% | 5-10% |
| Collectibles/other | 3% | 0% |

*Source: Knight Frank Wealth Report 2024, Vanguard How America Saves 2024*

Notice the difference. Wealthy investors hold roughly one-third of their portfolio in tangible assets (real estate, gold, collectibles) that exist outside the traditional brokerage system. The average 401(k) investor holds zero in those categories.

## The Roth Conversion Has Real Value -- But It Is Not Enough

To be clear: Roth conversions can be smart tax planning. Paying taxes now at a known rate versus later at an unknown (possibly higher) rate is a reasonable hedge. The couple in the MarketWatch article would likely benefit from some level of Roth conversion.

But the bigger question is whether optimizing within the same asset class (stocks and bonds, just in a different tax wrapper) addresses the real risks:

1. **Inflation risk:** The dollar has lost 23% of its purchasing power since 2019 (BLS CPI data)
2. **Sequence-of-returns risk:** A major market downturn in your first years of retirement can permanently reduce your income
3. **Policy risk:** Tax law changes can alter the value proposition of any retirement account
4. **Concentration risk:** Holding everything in paper financial assets means you are fully exposed to systemic financial disruptions

## A More Complete Approach

Rather than debating Traditional vs. Roth, consider diversifying across asset types:

- **Keep contributing to tax-advantaged accounts.** The employer match alone makes 401(k)s worthwhile.
- **Do the Roth conversion if the math works.** Paying a 24% tax rate now to avoid a potential 32%+ rate later is reasonable.
- **Allocate a portion to physical assets.** A self-directed IRA allows you to hold IRS-approved gold and silver with the same tax advantages. Gold returned 67% cumulatively from 2020-2024 (World Gold Council), providing genuine diversification during a period when bonds lost value in real terms.
- **Consider the role of tangible wealth.** Physical gold cannot be frozen by a bank, devalued by money printing, or eliminated by a legislative change.
