Representative Marjorie Taylor Greene's recent public warning about an "imminent Social Security crisis" brought renewed attention to a funding problem the Social Security Administration has been documenting for years. Regardless of where you stand politically, the numbers in the SSA's own reports paint a clear picture: the program's finances are under serious strain, and the clock is ticking.

Here's what the data actually shows and what it means for anyone planning to retire in the next 10-15 years.

## The Numbers Behind the Headlines

The 2024 Social Security Trustees Report lays out the math plainly:

| Metric | Figure |
|---|---|
| OASI trust fund depletion | 2033 |
| Combined trust fund depletion | 2035 |
| Benefits payable after depletion | ~83% of scheduled |
| 2024 program deficit | $45.1 billion |
| Beneficiaries (2024) | 67.9 million |
| Workers per beneficiary (2024) | 2.7 |
| Workers per beneficiary (1960) | 5.1 |

Social Security paid out **$1.35 trillion** in benefits in fiscal year 2024. It collected $1.31 trillion in payroll tax revenue. The $45 billion gap was covered by trust fund reserves. Those reserves currently sit at about $2.6 trillion, but they're shrinking every year.

## What Congressional Gridlock Means for Benefits

Congress has known about this problem for decades. The Government Accountability Office (GAO) has flagged Social Security's long-term solvency in every annual fiscal risk report since 2017. Despite this, no major reform legislation has advanced past committee in either chamber.

The political reality is straightforward:

- **Cutting benefits** is unpopular with voters 55+, who show up at the polls at the highest rate of any age group (65.2% turnout in 2024, per the Census Bureau)
- **Raising taxes** faces opposition from both parties, depending on which taxes get raised
- **Borrowing more** adds to a national debt that already exceeds $36 trillion (Treasury Department, January 2025)
- **Doing nothing** triggers the automatic 17% benefit cut when the trust fund hits zero

The Congressional Budget Office's December 2024 report projected that under current law, spending on Social Security will grow from 5.1% of GDP in 2024 to 6.1% by 2034. Something has to give.

## Who Gets Hit Hardest

Benefit cuts wouldn't hit everyone equally. SSA data from December 2024 shows:

- **Average retired worker benefit:** $1,907/month
- **Average spousal benefit:** $917/month
- **Average widow/widower benefit:** $1,773/month
- **Average disabled worker benefit:** $1,539/month

Workers who earned near the maximum taxable income throughout their careers would see the biggest dollar-amount cuts. But lower-income retirees who depend on Social Security for a larger share of their income face the toughest adjustments.

According to the SSA, Social Security represents **90% or more** of income for about 40% of beneficiaries aged 65+. A 17% cut for someone living almost entirely on a $1,500 monthly check means finding a way to survive on $1,245.

## What Previous "Fixes" Looked Like

The last major Social Security reform happened in 1983, when the program was about 6 months from insolvency. The Greenspan Commission's solution included:

1. Gradually raising full retirement age from 65 to 67
2. Making up to 50% of benefits taxable (later increased to 85% in 1993)
3. Increasing the payroll tax rate
4. Delaying cost-of-living adjustments by 6 months

Every fix involved retirees receiving less or paying more. People who'd been planning around the old rules had to adjust quickly.

## Practical Steps Regardless of Politics

Whether Congress acts this year, next year, or at the last minute like in 1983, the math points in the same direction: future retirees will likely receive less than currently promised, receive it later, or pay more for it.

Here's what that means in practical terms:

- **Run your numbers at ssa.gov.** Create a my Social Security account and see your projected benefit. Then calculate what 83% of that amount looks like.
- **Build income you control.** The SSA recommends treating Social Security as one leg of a three-legged stool: Social Security, employer retirement plans, and personal savings. If one leg gets shorter, the other two need to be stronger.
- **Consider tax-advantaged catch-up contributions.** If you're 50+, you can contribute an extra $7,500 to a 401(k) in 2025 and an extra $1,000 to an IRA.
- **Look at assets outside the stock-and-bond universe.** The World Gold Council reports that gold delivered 8.4% annualized returns over the 20-year period ending December 2024. During the 2008 financial crisis, when the S&P 500 dropped 37%, gold rose 5.5%. Physical gold in a self-directed IRA provides diversification that doesn't depend on corporate earnings or government solvency.

The political debate over Social Security will continue. What shouldn't wait is your own planning.
