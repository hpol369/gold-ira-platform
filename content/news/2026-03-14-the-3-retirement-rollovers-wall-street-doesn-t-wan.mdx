Most Americans think a retirement rollover means moving money from one 401(k) to another when they change jobs. That limited view keeps billions of dollars parked in high-fee accounts where advisors collect 1-2% annually whether your balance grows or not.

The IRS actually permits three rollover strategies that give you far more control over your retirement savings. Your broker has little incentive to mention them because each one moves your money out of their fee structure.

## Rollover #1: Self-Directed IRA

When you leave a job or retire, IRS rules let you roll your employer 401(k) into a self-directed IRA with zero taxes and zero penalties. The difference between a self-directed IRA and a standard IRA is what you can invest in.

A standard IRA at Fidelity or Schwab limits you to stocks, bonds, mutual funds, and ETFs. A self-directed IRA, held by a qualified custodian, opens the door to:

- **Physical gold and silver** (IRS-approved bullion per IRC Section 408(m)(3))
- **Real estate** (rental properties, commercial buildings, raw land)
- **Private lending** (promissory notes, trust deeds)
- **Private equity and LLCs**

According to the IRS, as of 2025 there are over 28 million traditional IRAs in the U.S. holding $13.4 trillion. The vast majority are invested solely in stocks and mutual funds. A self-directed rollover changes that equation entirely.

**Key rule:** The IRS requires your self-directed IRA assets be held by an approved custodian. You cannot store IRA gold in your home safe — that triggers a taxable distribution.

## Rollover #2: Roth Conversion

The Roth conversion lets you move pre-tax 401(k) or traditional IRA money into a Roth IRA. You pay income tax on the converted amount today, but every dollar of future growth is tax-free forever under IRC Section 408A.

The 2025 numbers make this worth serious consideration:

| Tax Bracket (Single) | Taxable Income | Federal Rate |
|---|---|---|
| 22% bracket | $47,151 - $100,525 | 22% |
| 24% bracket | $100,526 - $191,950 | 24% |
| 32% bracket | $191,951 - $243,725 | 32% |

With the national debt above $36 trillion as of March 2025 (U.S. Treasury data), and the Congressional Budget Office projecting sustained deficits through 2034, paying taxes at known rates today rather than unknown future rates is a concrete risk-management move.

**Practical example:** A 58-year-old retired factory supervisor with $400,000 in a traditional 401(k) could convert $50,000 per year over eight years. At the 22% bracket, that costs $11,000 in taxes per conversion — but locks in tax-free growth for the remaining decades of their retirement.

## Rollover #3: Solo 401(k) for Side Income

If you have any self-employment income — consulting, handyman work, driving for a delivery service, selling on Etsy — you can open a solo 401(k) under IRS rules.

The 2025 contribution limits are substantial:

- **Under 50:** Up to $70,000 total ($23,500 employee + employer profit-sharing)
- **Ages 50-59 and 64+:** Up to $77,500 (includes $7,500 catch-up)
- **Ages 60-63:** Up to $81,250 (enhanced catch-up of $11,250 under SECURE 2.0)

You can also roll existing 401(k) and IRA balances into your solo 401(k). Many solo 401(k) plans allow the same alternative investments as self-directed IRAs, including precious metals and real estate.

**Why it matters:** A 57-year-old nurse who picks up per diem shifts through an LLC could shelter a significant portion of that side income while also consolidating old retirement accounts into one self-directed plan.

## Why These Strategies Stay Under the Radar

The Investment Company Institute reports that Americans paid an estimated $126 billion in mutual fund fees in 2023. Every dollar that rolls out of a managed 401(k) into a self-directed account is a dollar that stops generating advisory fees.

That is not a conspiracy — it is a business model. Advisors who earn asset-based fees naturally steer clients toward options that keep assets under management.

## The Bottom Line

These three rollovers are legal, IRS-approved, and widely used by high-net-worth investors. They are not loopholes and they are not exotic. They simply require you to take an active role in managing your retirement.

**Your next step:** Pull up your most recent 401(k) statement. Look at the expense ratios on every fund. Add up the total fees as a dollar amount, not a percentage. That number is what you are paying annually for someone else to control your investment options.

If you decide that precious metals belong in your retirement mix, a self-directed IRA rollover is the tax-efficient way to make it happen.
