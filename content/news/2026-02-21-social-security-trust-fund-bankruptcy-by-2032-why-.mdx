The Social Security Board of Trustees dropped a number in their 2024 annual report that should concern every working American: the Old-Age and Survivors Insurance (OASI) trust fund will be depleted by **2033**. The combined OASI and Disability Insurance funds hit the wall by **2035**. Once that happens, incoming payroll taxes will only cover about **83%** of scheduled benefits, according to the Trustees' own projections.

That means an automatic, across-the-board benefit cut for every single recipient. No vote required. No warning letter in the mail. It just happens.

## What the Trustees Report Actually Says

Here are the key numbers from the 2024 Social Security Trustees Report:

| Metric | Figure |
|---|---|
| OASI trust fund depletion year | 2033 |
| Combined trust fund depletion year | 2035 |
| Benefit payable after depletion | 83% of scheduled amount |
| Current worker-to-beneficiary ratio | 2.7 workers per beneficiary |
| 1960 worker-to-beneficiary ratio | 5.1 workers per beneficiary |
| 2024 cost rate (% of taxable payroll) | 14.33% |
| 2024 income rate (% of taxable payroll) | 13.20% |

The math is simple. Social Security collects less in payroll taxes than it pays out in benefits. The gap is currently filled by drawing down the trust fund. When that fund hits zero, benefits get cut automatically under current law.

## Why This Is Happening

Three demographic forces are squeezing the system simultaneously:

1. **The Baby Boom retirement wave.** About 11,200 Americans turn 65 every day, according to the Census Bureau. The number of beneficiaries grew from 50 million in 2010 to over 67 million in 2024.

2. **Falling birth rates.** The U.S. total fertility rate dropped to 1.62 births per woman in 2023 (CDC National Vital Statistics), well below the 2.1 replacement rate. Fewer future workers means less payroll tax revenue.

3. **Longer lifespans.** When Social Security started in 1935, life expectancy at birth was 61 years. Today it's 77.5 years (CDC, 2023). The program was designed for a world where most people didn't collect benefits for long.

The Congressional Budget Office projects Social Security will run a cumulative cash deficit of **$4.3 trillion** over the next decade. That's not an opinion. That's their baseline forecast from the January 2024 Budget and Economic Outlook.

## What a 17% Cut Looks Like in Real Dollars

The average retired worker received **$1,907 per month** from Social Security as of January 2025, per the SSA. Here's what a 17% automatic cut does at different benefit levels:

| Current Monthly Benefit | After 17% Cut | Annual Loss |
|---|---|---|
| $1,500 | $1,245 | $3,060 |
| $1,907 (average) | $1,583 | $3,888 |
| $2,500 | $2,075 | $5,100 |
| $3,822 (maximum at FRA) | $3,172 | $7,800 |

Over 20 years of retirement, the average recipient loses roughly **$77,760**. For a married couple both collecting, the combined loss exceeds $155,000.

## What Congress Might Do (and What It Costs You)

The Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget has outlined the most commonly discussed fixes:

- **Raise the payroll tax cap.** Currently, earnings above $168,600 (2024) aren't subject to Social Security tax. Lifting or eliminating that cap would close about 70% of the shortfall.
- **Increase the retirement age.** Moving full retirement age from 67 to 69 would close about 20% of the gap but hit physical laborers hardest.
- **Reduce benefits for higher earners.** Means-testing would change the program's universal nature.
- **Increase the payroll tax rate.** A 1.6 percentage-point increase (split between employer and employee) would close the gap entirely.

Every option involves somebody paying more or getting less. The longer Congress waits, the more painful the fix becomes.

## Building a Backup Plan

Social Security was designed to replace about **40%** of pre-retirement income for average earners, according to the SSA. It was never meant to be your entire retirement. If you're 55 or older, here's what's worth considering:

- **Max out catch-up contributions.** Workers 50+ can contribute an extra $7,500 to their 401(k) in 2025, for a total of $30,500. IRA catch-up contributions add another $1,000 beyond the standard $7,000 limit.
- **Delay claiming if possible.** Each year you wait past 62 (up to 70) increases your benefit by roughly 6-8% per year. That's guaranteed income growth no stock can promise.
- **Diversify income sources.** The Bureau of Labor Statistics reports that retirees with three or more income sources have significantly lower poverty rates than those depending on Social Security alone.
- **Consider tangible assets.** Gold returned an annualized 8.4% over the 20-year period ending December 2024 (World Gold Council). Central banks added 1,037 tonnes to their reserves in 2023 alone, the second-highest annual total on record.

The trust fund math doesn't change based on who's in the White House. The demographic trends are baked in. What you can control is how much of your retirement depends on a single government program.
