The first year of retirement is when the biggest financial mistakes get locked in. A Yahoo Finance article recently outlined six money moves for new retirees, mostly standard advice about budgeting and insurance. But there are practical steps that go further, backed by data that the typical retirement playbook ignores.

Here are six moves worth making in your first 12 months of retirement, with the numbers behind each one.

## 1. Calculate Your Actual Spending, Not Your Estimated Spending

The Bureau of Labor Statistics 2023 Consumer Expenditure Survey found that households aged 65-74 spent an average of **$60,087 per year**. Here's where that money actually goes:

| Category | Annual Spending | % of Total |
|---|---|---|
| Housing | $19,900 | 33.1% |
| Transportation | $9,700 | 16.1% |
| Food | $7,500 | 12.5% |
| Healthcare | $7,200 | 12.0% |
| Entertainment | $3,200 | 5.3% |
| All other | $12,587 | 21.0% |

Most people underestimate healthcare. The Fidelity Retiree Health Care Cost Estimate (2024) projects that a 65-year-old couple retiring today will need approximately **$315,000** to cover healthcare expenses throughout retirement, after Medicare.

Track every dollar for the first three months. Your actual spending pattern will likely surprise you.

## 2. Set Up a Tax-Efficient Withdrawal Strategy

The order you pull money from different accounts can save you tens of thousands in taxes over a 20-year retirement. Here's the general framework:

- **First:** Draw from taxable brokerage accounts (capital gains rates, often lower)
- **Second:** Draw from traditional 401(k)/IRA (taxed as ordinary income)
- **Third:** Draw from Roth accounts (tax-free, let these grow longest)

The IRS requires **Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)** starting at age 73 under the SECURE 2.0 Act. For a $500,000 traditional IRA at age 73, the 2025 RMD would be roughly **$18,870** (using the IRS Uniform Lifetime Table divisor of 26.5).

Missing an RMD triggers a **25% penalty** on the amount not withdrawn (reduced from 50% under SECURE 2.0). That's real money lost to a paperwork mistake.

## 3. Delay Social Security If You Can

Each year you delay claiming Social Security past age 62 increases your benefit by **6-8% per year** until age 70. The SSA data makes the math clear:

| Claiming Age | Monthly Benefit (avg earner) | Annual Total |
|---|---|---|
| 62 | $1,336 | $16,032 |
| 65 | $1,620 | $19,440 |
| 67 (FRA) | $1,907 | $22,884 |
| 70 | $2,364 | $28,368 |

Source: SSA, January 2025 data for average earner.

The difference between claiming at 62 and 70 is **$12,336 per year** for the rest of your life. Over 20 years (age 70 to 90), that's **$246,720** in additional income.

If you have other income sources to bridge the gap from retirement to age 70, delaying is one of the highest-returning "investments" available. It's guaranteed by the federal government and indexed to inflation.

## 4. Review Your Fee Structure

The Department of Labor estimates that a 1% fee difference compounds to a **28% reduction** in your final account balance over 35 years. But fees matter in retirement too, when you're drawing down.

Check these costs in your current accounts:

- **Expense ratios** on mutual funds and ETFs (average equity fund: 0.44%, per the Investment Company Institute 2024 Fact Book; many target-date funds run 0.50-1.00%)
- **Advisory fees** (typical financial advisor: 0.75-1.25% of assets under management)
- **Trading commissions** (most major brokerages eliminated these, but some specialty platforms still charge)
- **Account maintenance fees** (some 401(k) plans charge $25-75/year)

On a $500,000 portfolio, a 1% annual advisory fee costs **$5,000/year**. Over 20 years of retirement, that's $100,000 in fees alone, not counting the lost growth on that money.

## 5. Diversify Beyond Stocks and Bonds

The standard 60/40 stock/bond portfolio returned an average of **7.5% annually** over the 30 years ending December 2024 (Vanguard data). But in 2022, both stocks AND bonds fell simultaneously. The S&P 500 dropped 19.4% while the Bloomberg Aggregate Bond Index lost 13.0%. That's the worst combined performance since 1969.

Adding assets with low stock-market correlation helps protect against years when the 60/40 model fails:

| Asset Class | 20-Year Return (annualized) | Correlation to S&P 500 |
|---|---|---|
| S&P 500 | 10.2% | 1.00 |
| U.S. Aggregate Bonds | 2.8% | -0.10 |
| Gold | 8.4% | 0.05 |
| REITs | 7.1% | 0.58 |

Source: World Gold Council, Morningstar, Bloomberg (through December 2024).

Gold's near-zero correlation to stocks means it tends to move independently. During the 2008 crisis, the S&P 500 fell 37% while gold rose 5.5%. Central banks added **1,037 tonnes** of gold to reserves in 2023 (World Gold Council), recognizing this diversification benefit at an institutional level.

## 6. Build a Cash Buffer

The sequence-of-returns risk is the most dangerous and least discussed threat to new retirees. If the market drops 30% in your first year and you're forced to sell investments to cover expenses, you permanently impair your portfolio's recovery potential.

Financial planners generally recommend keeping **12-24 months** of living expenses in cash or near-cash (money market funds, short-term CDs). Based on the BLS average of $60,087 in annual spending for 65-74 households, that means **$60,000-$120,000** in liquid reserves.

This cash buffer lets you ride out market downturns without selling stocks or bonds at depressed prices. It's boring, but it's effective.

## Putting It All Together

The first year of retirement sets the trajectory for everything that follows. Getting the tax strategy right, understanding your actual costs, and building a portfolio that can handle bad years as well as good ones matters more than chasing returns ever did.
