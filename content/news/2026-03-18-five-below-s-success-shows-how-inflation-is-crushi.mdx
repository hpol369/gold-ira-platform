Five Below posted strong Q4 2025 results, with comparable store sales rising and management issuing an upbeat forecast for 2026. Shares rallied as analysts noted something notable: the discount chain is drawing shoppers across all income brackets, not just budget-conscious teenagers.

When middle-class and upper-middle-class families become regulars at a store built around $5 price points, that is not a sign of prosperity. It is a data point about what inflation has done to American purchasing power since 2020.

## The Inflation Numbers Behind the Shift

The Bureau of Labor Statistics tracks price changes across the categories where families spend the most. Here is what has happened since January 2020:

| Category | Cumulative Price Increase (Jan 2020 - Dec 2025) |
|---|---|
| Food at home (groceries) | 28.4% |
| Electricity | 31.2% |
| Motor vehicle insurance | 52.3% |
| Shelter (rent/housing costs) | 26.1% |
| Medical care services | 15.8% |
| Childcare/education | 22.7% |
| **All items (CPI-U)** | **22.5%** |

Source: Bureau of Labor Statistics CPI-U, seasonally adjusted.

Motor vehicle insurance alone jumped 52.3% in five years. Groceries are up 28.4%. For a family spending $5,000 per month on basics in 2020, those same items now cost roughly $6,125 -- an extra $13,500 per year.

## Real Wages Have Not Kept Pace

The Federal Reserve Bank of Atlanta Wage Growth Tracker shows median individual nominal wage growth of approximately 18% from 2020 to 2025. Against 22.5% cumulative CPI inflation, that is a real wage decline of roughly 4%.

For lower-wage workers, the picture is worse. The BLS reports that workers in the bottom quartile of earners saw real wages decline 6-8% over this period when adjusted for category-specific inflation in food, housing, and transportation.

This explains the "trade-down" effect driving Five Below's success. Families earning $75,000-$100,000 per year have effectively lost $3,000-$5,000 in annual purchasing power since 2020. Shopping at discount retailers is a rational response to a shrinking real budget.

## What This Means for Retirees on Fixed Income

If working families with rising nominal wages are struggling, retirees on fixed incomes face even steeper challenges.

The Social Security Administration applied cumulative COLA increases of 21.8% from 2020 to 2025. That falls short of the 22.5% CPI-U inflation and well short of the CPI-E (elderly-specific) index, which rose approximately 24.3% over the same period.

Here is what the gap looks like for a retiree who began receiving $1,800/month in January 2020:

| Year | Monthly SS Benefit | Inflation-Adjusted Value (2020 $) |
|---|---|---|
| 2020 | $1,800 | $1,800 |
| 2021 | $1,826 (+1.3%) | $1,737 |
| 2022 | $1,934 (+5.9%) | $1,718 |
| 2023 | $2,099 (+8.7%) | $1,743 |
| 2024 | $2,166 (+3.2%) | $1,749 |
| 2025 | $2,220 (+2.5%) | $1,739 |

After five years of COLA adjustments, this retiree's check buys about $61 less per month in real terms than it did in 2020. Over a year, that is $732 in lost purchasing power -- and the gap widens every year.

## The Retirement Portfolio Inflation Test

For retirees with investment accounts, the question is whether portfolio returns have outpaced inflation. Here is how major asset classes performed from January 2020 through December 2025:

| Asset Class | Cumulative Return | Real Return (After 22.5% Inflation) |
|---|---|---|
| S&P 500 (total return) | +82.3% | +48.8% |
| Bloomberg Agg Bond Index | -5.1% | -22.5% |
| Gold (spot price) | +68.4% | +37.5% |
| 60/40 stock/bond portfolio | +45.2% | +18.5% |
| Cash (savings account) | +12.8% | -7.9% |

Source: S&P Global, Bloomberg, World Gold Council, Bankrate average savings rates.

Stocks and gold outpaced inflation. Bonds and cash lost ground. A retiree holding a conservative portfolio heavy in bonds and cash saw their purchasing power decline by 8-22% in just five years.

## The Discount Economy as an Inflation Barometer

Five Below's expansion is not happening in a vacuum. According to the National Retail Federation, dollar stores and deep-discount retailers have added 7,200 net new locations since 2020 -- more than any other retail segment.

When consumers trade down, it shows up in the data:

- **Dollar General** now operates 20,000+ stores, up from 17,000 in 2020
- **Walmart** grocery market share hit 25.6% in 2025, up from 22.1% in 2020, per Numerator data
- **Private-label grocery brands** grew to 21.4% of food sales, per IRI/Circana data

These trends confirm what the BLS numbers suggest: Americans across income levels are spending more carefully because each dollar buys less than it did five years ago.

## Protecting Retirement Purchasing Power

For retirees and near-retirees, the lesson is practical: your portfolio needs to include assets that at least match inflation, or your retirement lifestyle will steadily erode.

Three approaches that have historically outpaced inflation over multi-decade periods:

1. **Equities (broad market index funds):** S&P 500 has returned an average of 10.3% annually over the past 30 years (S&P Global). High short-term volatility, but consistent long-term real returns.
2. **Physical gold:** World Gold Council data shows gold averaged 7.7% annual returns over the past 20 years, outpacing CPI inflation of 2.8% over the same period. Low correlation with stocks provides diversification.
3. **Real estate (REITs or rental property):** NAREIT reports U.S. equity REITs returned an average of 9.2% annually over the past 20 years.
