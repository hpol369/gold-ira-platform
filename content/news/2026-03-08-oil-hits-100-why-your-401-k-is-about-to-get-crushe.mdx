Crude oil crossed $100 per barrel for the first time since June 2022, driven by the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz as the Iran conflict intensified. Yahoo Finance reported that major producers in the Persian Gulf region are being forced to shut in operations, removing millions of barrels from global supply.

This is not a brief price spike. With the strait effectively closed -- a scenario energy analysts had long warned about -- the supply disruption could persist for weeks or months. For anyone within 10 years of retirement, the economic consequences deserve serious attention.

## What $100 Oil Actually Means

The Strait of Hormuz handles approximately 21 million barrels of oil per day (Energy Information Administration). Its closure forces tankers to reroute around Africa, adding 10-15 days and roughly $3-5 per barrel in shipping costs alone.

Here is how $100 oil translates into everyday costs:

| Item | Price at $75 Oil | Estimated Price at $100 Oil | Increase |
|---|---|---|---|
| Gallon of gasoline | $3.25 | $4.00-$4.25 | +23-31% |
| Home heating oil (per gallon) | $3.80 | $4.70-$5.00 | +24-32% |
| Cross-country shipping (per load) | $2,800 | $3,400-$3,600 | +21-29% |
| Electricity (nat. gas regions, per kWh) | $0.16 | $0.18-$0.20 | +13-25% |
| Airline ticket (domestic avg.) | $340 | $395-$420 | +16-24% |

*Sources: EIA, AAA, American Trucking Associations, Airlines for America*

These are direct costs. The indirect effects -- higher manufacturing costs, shipping surcharges passed through to consumer goods, agricultural input cost increases -- take 2-4 months to fully appear in retail prices.

## Oil Shocks and Recession: The Historical Record

James Hamilton, economics professor at UC San Diego, documented in a widely cited 2003 paper that 10 of the 11 post-WWII recessions were preceded by oil price spikes. The Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis updated this analysis in 2023, confirming the pattern holds.

| Recession | Oil Price Increase (Prior 12 Mo.) | GDP Decline | Unemployment Peak |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1973-1975 | +300% | -3.2% | 9.0% |
| 1980 | +150% | -2.2% | 7.8% |
| 1990-1991 | +90% | -1.4% | 7.8% |
| 2007-2009 | +94% | -4.3% | 10.0% |

*Sources: FRED, Bureau of Economic Analysis, Hamilton (2003)*

The current move from roughly $70 to $100 represents a 43% increase. If the strait remains closed and prices push toward $120-$130, the increase would reach 70-85% from recent lows -- well within the range that has historically triggered recessions.

## Impact on a Typical Pre-Retirement Portfolio

Vanguard's 2024 How America Saves report shows the average 401(k) balance for Americans aged 55-64 is $207,874. Here is what various oil scenarios mean for that portfolio:

**Scenario 1: Oil at $100, brief disruption (3-6 months)**
- S&P 500 impact: -5% to -10%
- Bond fund impact: -2% to -5%
- Estimated portfolio loss: $10,000-$18,000
- Plus purchasing power loss from 4-5% inflation: $8,000-$10,000/year

**Scenario 2: Oil at $120+, prolonged disruption (6-12 months)**
- S&P 500 impact: -15% to -25%
- Bond fund impact: -5% to -10%
- Estimated portfolio loss: $30,000-$52,000
- Plus purchasing power loss from 6-8% inflation: $12,000-$17,000/year

**Scenario 3: Oil returns to $75 within 3 months**
- Limited portfolio impact
- Temporary inflation bump of 1-2%
- Modest portfolio recovery

The uncomfortable reality: scenarios 1 and 2 are more likely than scenario 3, given that the Strait of Hormuz has never been successfully blockaded for an extended period, meaning there is no historical precedent for how long recovery takes.

## What Central Banks Are Doing (and What That Tells You)

While individual investors debate whether to sell stocks, central banks have been making a very clear statement with their gold purchases:

- 2022: Central banks bought 1,082 tons of gold (World Gold Council) -- the highest annual total since 1950
- 2023: Central banks bought 1,037 tons
- 2024 (through Q3): Central banks bought 694 tons, on pace for another 900+ ton year
- Top buyers: China (PBOC), Poland, Turkey, India, Czech Republic

Central banks are the most informed institutional actors in the global economy. Their sustained gold buying suggests they are preparing for exactly the kind of monetary and geopolitical instability that $100+ oil represents.

## Practical Steps

1. **Do not panic-sell.** Selling stocks during a crisis locks in losses. The goal is to be diversified before the crisis, not to react during it.

2. **Review your allocation honestly.** If your portfolio is 100% stocks and bonds, you have zero exposure to assets that historically benefit from oil-driven inflation. That is a concentration risk.

3. **Consider a partial rollover.** If you are 55+ and your employer plan allows in-service distributions, you can move a portion to a self-directed IRA without waiting to retire. This opens access to physical gold and silver -- the asset class that central banks themselves are buying.

4. **Size the position appropriately.** Most financial planners who include gold recommend 5-15% of total portfolio value. That is enough to provide meaningful protection without abandoning your core investment strategy.
