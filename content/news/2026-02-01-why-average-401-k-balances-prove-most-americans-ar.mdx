Fidelity Investments released its Q3 2024 retirement analysis covering 46 million 401(k) accounts, and the numbers confirm what a lot of working Americans already suspect: most people aren't anywhere close to where they need to be.

The average 401(k) balance for workers in their 60s came in at **$112,500**. The median (a better measure, since it strips out the millionaire accounts pulling the average up) was roughly **$71,000**. For workers in their 50s, the average sat at **$60,900**.

These aren't made-up numbers from a finance blog. This is Fidelity's own data from the largest pool of 401(k) accounts in the country.

## How the Balances Stack Up by Age

Fidelity's Q3 2024 data broke down average and median balances across age groups:

| Age Group | Average Balance | Median Balance |
|---|---|---|
| 20s | $16,500 | $7,100 |
| 30s | $40,200 | $18,400 |
| 40s | $63,000 | $31,200 |
| 50s | $60,900 | $28,900 |
| 60s | $112,500 | $71,000 |
| 65+ | $102,900 | $55,800 |

Notice the drop-off after 60. Many workers start withdrawing or rolling over funds as they approach retirement, which pulls balances down. But even the peak average of $112,500 falls well short of what most financial planning benchmarks suggest.

## What Financial Planners Say You Need vs. What People Actually Have

Fidelity's own retirement guideline recommends having **10x your annual salary** saved by age 67. The Bureau of Labor Statistics reports the median weekly earnings for full-time workers aged 55-64 was **$1,180** in Q3 2024, or roughly $61,360 per year.

By Fidelity's own benchmark, a 65-year-old earning $61,000 should have about **$610,000** saved. The actual median balance of $55,800 for 65+ accounts represents about **9% of that target**.

| Age | Fidelity Savings Target | Actual Median | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| 50 | 6x salary ($368K) | $28,900 | -$339,100 |
| 60 | 8x salary ($491K) | $71,000 | -$420,000 |
| 67 | 10x salary ($614K) | $55,800 | -$558,200 |

The gap is enormous. And it doesn't account for the fact that many Americans have no 401(k) at all. The Federal Reserve's 2022 Survey of Consumer Finances found that only **54.3%** of working-age families had any retirement account.

## Why the 4% Rule Tells the Real Story

Financial planners commonly use the "4% rule," which says you can withdraw 4% of your savings in year one of retirement, then adjust for inflation each year, with a reasonable expectation your money lasts 30 years.

Here's what that produces at actual account balances:

| 401(k) Balance | Annual Withdrawal (4%) | Monthly Income |
|---|---|---|
| $55,800 (median, 65+) | $2,232 | $186 |
| $71,000 (median, 60s) | $2,840 | $237 |
| $112,500 (average, 60s) | $4,500 | $375 |
| $610,000 (Fidelity target) | $24,400 | $2,033 |

Even at the average balance, $375 per month from your 401(k) barely covers a car payment. Add the average Social Security benefit of $1,907/month (SSA, January 2025), and you're looking at roughly **$2,282/month** total. That's $27,384 per year.

The BLS reports that average annual expenditures for households aged 65-74 were **$60,087** in 2023. The math doesn't add up.

## What's Eating Into Savings

Several factors compound the problem:

- **Fees.** The Department of Labor estimates that a 1% difference in fees over a 35-year career reduces your final balance by about **28%**. The Investment Company Institute reports the average 401(k) equity mutual fund expense ratio was 0.44% in 2023, but many plans still carry funds above 1%.
- **Cash-outs.** About **40%** of workers who leave a job cash out their 401(k) instead of rolling it over, according to the Employee Benefit Research Institute. Early withdrawals before age 59 1/2 trigger a 10% penalty plus income taxes.
- **Late starts.** The Center for Retirement Research at Boston College estimates that the median household doesn't begin saving for retirement until age **31-35**, missing the most powerful years of compound growth.
- **Inflation erosion.** The BLS CPI shows cumulative inflation of 22.7% between January 2020 and December 2024. A dollar saved in 2020 buys about 77 cents worth of goods today.

## What You Can Actually Do About It

If you're 50 or older and behind on savings, the IRS provides catch-up provisions worth using:

- **401(k) catch-up:** Workers 50+ can contribute an extra $7,500 in 2025, for a total limit of **$30,500**. Starting in 2025, workers aged 60-63 get a "super catch-up" of $11,250 extra under the SECURE 2.0 Act.
- **IRA catch-up:** An additional $1,000 beyond the standard $7,000 limit for those 50+.
- **HSA contributions:** If you have a high-deductible health plan, the 2025 HSA limit is $4,300 (individual) or $8,550 (family) with a $1,000 catch-up for 55+.

Beyond maxing contributions, diversification matters. The Federal Reserve's data shows that Americans with three or more asset types in their retirement portfolio had significantly higher median net worth than those concentrated in a single category.

Physical gold returned an annualized **8.4%** over the 20 years ending December 2024 (World Gold Council). During the 2008 financial crisis, when U.S. stocks lost 37%, gold gained 5.5%.
