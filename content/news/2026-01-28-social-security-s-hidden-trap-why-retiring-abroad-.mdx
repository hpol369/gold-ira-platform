Roughly 700,000 Americans currently collect Social Security while living abroad, according to the SSA's Office of Research. A recent MarketWatch question from a couple planning to retire in Mexico exposed a problem most of them never saw coming: will a surviving spouse actually keep those benefits if one partner dies overseas?

The short answer depends on citizenship. The long answer could cost your family thousands of dollars a month.

## How Survivor Benefits Work Outside the U.S.

The Social Security Administration applies different rules depending on citizenship status and country of residence:

| Situation | Survivor Benefits Status |
|---|---|
| U.S. citizen spouse, any country | Generally receives full benefits |
| Non-citizen spouse in a treaty country (e.g., Canada, UK, Germany) | Eligible under Totalization Agreement |
| Non-citizen spouse in a non-treaty country | May lose benefits after 6 months outside U.S. |
| Non-citizen spouse in a restricted country (Cuba, North Korea, etc.) | Benefits suspended entirely |

The SSA maintains Totalization Agreements with 30 countries as of 2025, covering most of Western Europe, Canada, Australia, Japan, and South Korea. Mexico is notably **not** on that list. For a non-citizen spouse living in Mexico, the SSA's "alien nonpayment provision" under Section 202(t) of the Social Security Act can reduce or eliminate survivor benefits.

## The Numbers That Matter

Here's why this hits harder than most people realize:

- **Average Social Security survivor benefit:** $1,773/month (SSA, December 2024)
- **Maximum survivor benefit at full retirement age:** $3,822/month (SSA, 2025)
- **Over a 20-year retirement:** Losing $1,773/month adds up to $425,520 in lost income

The SSA paid $7.4 billion to beneficiaries outside the United States in fiscal year 2023. But that money comes with strings. If your spouse doesn't meet the citizenship or residency requirements, those payments can stop with a single administrative determination.

## Who This Affects Most

This issue hits hardest for couples where:

- One spouse is a U.S. citizen and the other is not
- They plan to retire in Latin America, Southeast Asia, or other popular low-cost destinations without Totalization Agreements
- The higher-earning spouse (whose record determines survivor benefits) dies first
- The surviving non-citizen spouse has limited work history of their own

According to the Census Bureau's 2023 American Community Survey, about 3.6 million married couples in the U.S. include at least one foreign-born spouse. Many of these couples dream of retiring abroad where their dollars stretch further.

## Steps to Protect Your Household

**1. Check your country's Totalization Agreement status.** Visit ssa.gov/international to see the current list of 30 treaty countries. If your destination isn't listed, plan accordingly.

**2. Understand the 5-year residency rule.** Non-citizen spouses who lived in the U.S. for at least 5 years during the marriage may still qualify under certain conditions. Document everything.

**3. Know the 6-month rule.** Non-citizen beneficiaries outside the U.S. for more than 6 consecutive months may see benefits suspended. Brief return trips to the U.S. can reset this clock.

**4. Run your numbers on ssa.gov.** Create a my Social Security account and check your estimated survivor benefits. Know exactly what's at stake before you make the move.

**5. Build income streams you control.** The core problem here is depending on a single government program for retirement income. The SSA's own 2024 Trustees Report projects the combined trust funds will be depleted by 2035, triggering automatic benefit cuts of roughly 17% for all recipients, foreign or domestic.
