A 75-year-old man on oxygen, in poor health, with less than $20,000 in savings and no income beyond Social Security. That's the situation described in a recent letter to MarketWatch, and the numbers tell a story that applies to far more Americans than most people realize.

This isn't an unusual case. According to the Federal Reserve's 2022 Survey of Consumer Finances, roughly 25% of Americans aged 75+ have less than $25,000 in total financial assets outside of their home. Here's why that happens and what you can do right now to avoid it.

## How $20,000 and Social Security Actually Works at 75

Let's put real numbers on this man's situation:

- **Average Social Security benefit at 75:** approximately $2,050/month (SSA data, accounting for COLAs since initial claim)
- **Annual income from Social Security:** roughly $24,600
- **Savings cushion:** $20,000

At current spending rates for a 75-year-old, that $20,000 buffer would be gone in months if a single major expense hits. Here's what retirees in this age bracket actually face:

| Expense Category | Average Annual Cost (Age 75+) | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Healthcare (out-of-pocket + premiums) | $7,030 | CMS / Medicare.gov |
| Housing (for homeowners, including maintenance) | $14,870 | BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey 2023 |
| Food | $5,490 | BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey 2023 |
| Transportation | $5,380 | BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey 2023 |
| **Total basic expenses** | **$32,770** | |

His Social Security covers $24,600. That leaves a gap of roughly $8,170 per year just for basics. At that rate, his $20,000 savings last about 2.4 years before he's entirely dependent on his monthly check alone.

And that assumes no emergencies. A single hospital stay with Medicare copays can run $5,000-$15,000 depending on the length and services. A car breakdown. A dental procedure Medicare doesn't cover. Any one event accelerates the timeline.

## Why This Keeps Happening

This man's situation reflects three converging problems that affect millions of retirees:

**1. Social Security was never meant to be your whole income.** The SSA's own retirement planner states that benefits are designed to replace approximately 40% of pre-retirement earnings for average wage earners. Yet according to the SSA, about 40% of unmarried elderly beneficiaries rely on Social Security for 90% or more of their income.

**2. Traditional savings vehicles haven't kept pace with real costs.** From 2000 to 2025, the average savings account interest rate ranged from 0.01% to about 4.5% (FDIC data). During that same period, cumulative inflation exceeded 80% per BLS data. Someone who kept $100,000 in savings accounts for 25 years lost significant purchasing power even while their balance grew slightly.

**3. Healthcare costs are the wildcard.** Fidelity's 2025 Retiree Health Care Cost Estimate puts the average 65-year-old couple's lifetime healthcare costs at $315,000 after Medicare. For a single person, it's roughly $165,000. That number doesn't include long-term care, which averages $108,405 per year for a private nursing home room according to Genworth's 2024 Cost of Care Survey.

## The Numbers You Need to Know Right Now

If you're between 55 and 70, here's where things stand nationally:

- **Median retirement savings (ages 55-64):** $185,000 (Federal Reserve SCF 2022)
- **Median retirement savings (ages 65-74):** $200,000 (Federal Reserve SCF 2022)
- **Social Security trust fund projected depletion:** 2035, after which payroll taxes cover about 83% of scheduled benefits (2024 Trustees Report)
- **Average 401(k) balance (ages 60-69):** $224,100 (Vanguard How America Saves 2024)
- **4% rule annual income from $224,100:** $8,964/year

Even the "average" near-retiree with a 401(k) is looking at Social Security plus roughly $9,000 per year from savings. That's better than the 75-year-old in this story, but it's not comfortable by any measure.

## Four Steps to Avoid This Outcome

**1. Calculate your actual retirement gap.** Add up your expected Social Security benefit (check at ssa.gov/myaccount), your projected retirement account withdrawals at 4%, and any pension or other income. Compare that to your current spending minus work-related expenses. The difference is your gap. Be honest about it.

**2. Maximize catch-up contributions while you can.** If you're 50 or older, the IRS allows an extra $7,500 per year into a 401(k) (total limit $31,000 for 2026) and an extra $1,000 into an IRA (total limit $8,000 for 2026). For workers aged 60-63, the SECURE 2.0 Act allows an even higher 401(k) catch-up of $11,250, bringing the total to $34,750.

**3. Diversify beyond paper assets.** The sequence-of-returns risk — a market crash right before or early in retirement — is what devastates portfolios. The S&P 500 dropped 37% in 2008 and 18% in 2022. Gold, by contrast, rose 5.5% in 2008 and held essentially flat in 2022, per World Gold Council data. A self-directed IRA lets you hold physical precious metals alongside traditional investments. The goal isn't to go all-in on any one asset, but to spread risk across things that don't all drop at the same time.

**4. Plan specifically for healthcare costs.** If you're still working and have access to a Health Savings Account (HSA), max it out ($4,300 individual / $8,550 family for 2026, plus $1,000 catch-up if 55+). HSA funds grow tax-free and can be used for medical expenses at any age. After 65, they work like a traditional IRA for non-medical expenses.

## The Takeaway

The 75-year-old in this story didn't fail because he was lazy or foolish. He likely followed conventional advice in a system that wasn't designed to fully support him. Social Security replaces a fraction of working income. Traditional savings accounts lost ground to inflation for a quarter century. And healthcare costs escalated faster than anyone projected.

The lesson isn't "you should have done better." The lesson is that relying on any single income source in retirement is structurally risky. The people who maintain their standard of living past 75 tend to have income from multiple places: Social Security, retirement accounts, and assets that hold value across economic cycles.
