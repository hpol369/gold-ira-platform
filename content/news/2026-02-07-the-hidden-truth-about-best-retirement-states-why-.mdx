Every year, financial publications roll out their "best states for retirees" lists. Yahoo Finance's latest version highlights Florida, Texas, Tennessee, and a few others where taxes are lower and the cost of living is friendlier. There is some practical value in these lists. But they leave out the single biggest threat to your retirement savings -- and it follows you to every zip code in America.

## What the "Best States" Lists Actually Show

Here is a comparison of the states most frequently recommended for retirees, based on 2024-2025 data:

| State | State Income Tax | Median Home Price | Avg. Monthly Expenses (Retiree) | Sales Tax |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Florida | 0% | $404,100 | $3,890 | 6% |
| Texas | 0% | $301,000 | $3,620 | 6.25% |
| Tennessee | 0% (on wages) | $309,800 | $3,340 | 7% |
| Nevada | 0% | $425,000 | $3,780 | 6.85% |
| Wyoming | 0% | $315,000 | $3,510 | 4% |
| California | Up to 13.3% | $793,600 | $5,120 | 7.25% |

*Sources: U.S. Census Bureau, Bureau of Labor Statistics, Tax Foundation, Zillow 2024*

Moving from California to Tennessee could save a retiree roughly $1,780 per month, or about $21,360 per year. That is real money.

## What the Lists Leave Out

The problem is not the savings. The problem is that inflation erodes those savings regardless of where you live.

According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, the Consumer Price Index rose 19.3% cumulatively from January 2020 to December 2024. Some categories hit retirees especially hard:

- **Groceries:** Up 25.4% since 2020 (BLS Food-at-Home Index)
- **Health insurance:** Medicare Part B premiums rose from $144.60/month in 2020 to $174.70/month in 2024 -- a 21% increase
- **Homeowners insurance:** Up 33% nationally between 2020 and 2024 (Insurance Information Institute), with Florida averaging 42% increases
- **Property taxes:** Even no-income-tax states have to fund services somehow. Texas property tax rates average 1.68%, among the highest in the nation (Tax Foundation)
- **Utilities:** Electricity costs rose 29% nationally from 2020-2024 (EIA data)

So you save $21,000 by moving to Tennessee, but inflation takes back $20,000-$30,000 annually in purchasing power from your entire nest egg. The math does not work if your savings are parked in assets that lose value every year.

## The Inflation Problem Nobody Talks About

A retiree with $500,000 in a savings account earning 4.5% APY collects about $22,500 per year in interest. Sounds decent. But if real inflation (including housing, food, and medical costs) runs closer to 6-7% for retirees -- which the BLS's CPI-E (Experimental Consumer Price Index for the Elderly) consistently shows -- that same retiree loses $30,000-$35,000 in purchasing power annually.

Net result: you are falling behind by $7,500 to $12,500 per year, no matter which state you live in.

The Federal Reserve's balance sheet expanded from $4.2 trillion in early 2020 to a peak of $8.9 trillion by early 2022. Even after some reduction, it remains above $7 trillion. That money supply expansion is a direct driver of the price increases retirees face daily.

## A Smarter Approach: Diversify What You Own, Not Just Where You Live

Geographic relocation can save you money on taxes. That is a legitimate strategy. But it does not address the core threat: holding 100% of your retirement in dollar-denominated assets during a period of persistent inflation.

Here is how different asset classes performed during the high-inflation period of 2020-2024:

| Asset | Cumulative Return (2020-2024) | Real Return (After ~19% Inflation) |
|---|---|---|
| Gold | +67% | +48% |
| S&P 500 | +82% | +63% |
| 10-Year Treasury Bond | -8% | -27% |
| Savings Account (avg) | +12% | -7% |
| Silver | +47% | +28% |

*Sources: World Gold Council, Federal Reserve, S&P Global, LBMA*

The retirees who preserved the most purchasing power were those who held a mix of assets -- not just stocks, and not just cash. Physical gold and silver performed well specifically because they tend to hold value when currencies weaken.

## Practical Takeaways

1. **If a low-tax state makes sense for your lifestyle, go for it.** The tax savings are real.
2. **But do not stop there.** The bigger lever is what you own, not where you live.
3. **Look at your portfolio honestly.** If 100% of your retirement is in cash, bonds, or a single 401(k) fund, you are more exposed to inflation than you probably realize.
4. **Consider adding physical assets.** A self-directed IRA allows you to hold gold and silver with the same tax benefits as a traditional retirement account.
