Merck's recent headlines as a "retirement stock" highlight a strategy millions of Americans rely on: buy dividend-paying blue chips, collect quarterly checks, and coast through retirement. On paper, Merck's 3.2% yield and 13 consecutive years of dividend increases (as of January 2025) look solid.

But dividend-only strategies carry risks that rarely make it into the sales pitch. And the historical record shows why putting all your retirement eggs in one basket, even a blue-chip basket, deserves a hard second look.

## The Track Record Wall Street Glosses Over

Dividend cuts happen more often than most investors realize. S&P Global data shows that during the 2020 COVID downturn, **191 S&P 1500 companies** cut or suspended their dividends. During the 2008-2009 financial crisis, the number was even higher.

Here are some "safe" dividend stocks that slashed payouts when retirees needed them most:

| Company | Year Cut | Dividend Reduction |
|---|---|---|
| General Electric | 2009 | Cut 68% |
| Bank of America | 2009 | Cut 97% |
| Ford Motor | 2020 | Suspended entirely |
| Wells Fargo | 2020 | Cut 80% |
| Disney | 2020 | Suspended entirely |
| AT&T | 2022 | Cut 47% |

Every one of these was considered a "blue-chip dividend stock" before the cut. GE had paid dividends continuously since 1899 before slashing in 2009.

## The Math on a Dividend-Only Retirement

Let's say you've built a $500,000 portfolio of dividend stocks yielding 3.5% on average. That generates **$17,500 per year**, or about **$1,458 per month** before taxes.

The BLS Consumer Price Index shows cumulative inflation of 22.7% between January 2020 and December 2024. If your dividends grew at only 2-3% annually during that stretch (which was typical for many dividend aristocrats), your purchasing power actually declined.

Here's how dividend income erodes over a 15-year retirement at different inflation rates:

| Year | Annual Income (3% growth) | Purchasing Power (4% inflation) | Real Value Loss |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | $17,500 | $17,500 | $0 |
| 5 | $19,700 | $16,200 | -$3,500 |
| 10 | $22,800 | $13,900 | -$8,900 |
| 15 | $26,400 | $11,900 | -$14,500 |

The dollar amount goes up. What it buys goes down. After 15 years, your dividend check buys about 32% less than when you started.

## Concentration Risk Is Real

The Federal Reserve's 2022 Survey of Consumer Finances found that among households headed by someone 65-74 with stock holdings, the **median stock portfolio** was roughly $250,000. Many retirees concentrate heavily in a handful of familiar names.

Single-stock risk is especially dangerous in retirement because:

- **You can't wait it out.** A 40-year-old can ride out a 50% drawdown. A 68-year-old selling shares for living expenses locks in losses permanently.
- **Sector shocks are unpredictable.** Pharmaceutical companies face patent cliffs, regulatory changes, and litigation. Merck's Keytruda (representing 45% of revenue) faces biosimilar competition starting in 2028.
- **Dividend yields can be misleading.** A rising yield sometimes signals a falling stock price, not increasing generosity.

The Fidelity Investments Q3 2024 retirement analysis found that only **29%** of 401(k) participants with more than $1 million were adequately diversified across asset classes.

## What Diversification Actually Looks Like

Financial researchers have studied this extensively. The Ibbotson Associates data (now part of Morningstar) going back to 1926 shows that no single asset class outperforms in every environment:

- **Stocks** perform best during economic expansion
- **Bonds** tend to hold up during deflation
- **Gold** has historically performed well during high inflation and currency devaluation
- **Real estate** provides income and inflation protection over long periods

The World Gold Council's research shows gold delivered an annualized return of **8.4%** over the 20-year period ending December 2024. During the 2008 financial crisis, when the S&P 500 dropped 37%, gold rose 5.5%.

Central banks understand this. They added **1,037 tonnes of gold** to reserves in 2023 (World Gold Council), the second-highest annual figure on record. China's central bank purchased gold for 18 consecutive months through late 2024.

## A Practical Approach

Nobody is saying to dump all your dividend stocks tomorrow. But building retirement income around a single strategy leaves you exposed when that strategy hits a rough patch, and every strategy eventually does.

A few concrete steps worth considering:

1. **Stress-test your portfolio.** Look at what happened to your holdings during 2008, 2020, and 2022. If the same drop happened next year, could you still cover your bills?
2. **Check your concentration.** If any single stock makes up more than 10% of your retirement portfolio, that's a red flag.
3. **Consider assets that move differently than stocks.** Physical gold and silver have historically low correlation to the S&P 500 (about 0.05 over 30 years, per the World Gold Council).
4. **Look at after-inflation returns, not just nominal yields.** A 3.5% dividend with 4% inflation is a negative real return.
