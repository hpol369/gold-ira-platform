Parents are opening Roth IRAs for children as young as 8 years old, and the math behind it is genuinely impressive. But this strategy has specific IRS rules most families get wrong, and it raises a bigger question: if early compounding is this powerful, what can someone in their 50s or 60s still do?

Here's how kid Roth IRAs actually work, the real numbers, and what this trend means if you're closer to retirement than to kindergarten.

## The IRS Rules for a Child's Roth IRA

A child of any age can have a Roth IRA, but they must have earned income. According to IRS Publication 590-A, earned income includes wages, salaries, tips, and net self-employment income. It does not include allowance, gifts, or investment returns.

Common sources of qualifying earned income for minors:

- Babysitting or lawn mowing (self-employment income, reported on Schedule C)
- Acting, modeling, or social media work
- Working at a family business (legitimate wages at fair market rates)
- Part-time jobs for teens 14+

**The 2026 Roth IRA contribution limit is $7,000** (or the child's total earned income, whichever is less). There is no minimum age requirement. A custodial Roth IRA is opened by the parent, with the child as the account holder.

**Key rule:** The child must actually earn the money. The IRS can disqualify contributions if the income isn't legitimate. Paying your 8-year-old $7,000 to "clean their room" won't hold up in an audit. But paying them a reasonable hourly rate for real work in a family business is perfectly legal.

## The Compounding Math, Done Honestly

Here's what makes this strategy compelling, using conservative and realistic assumptions:

| Scenario | Annual Contribution | Years Contributing | Growth Rate | Balance at Age 65 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Child starts at 8, contributes to 18 | $2,000/year for 10 years | 10 | 7% (historical stock market average minus fees) | ~$570,000 |
| Child starts at 8, contributes to 18 | $5,000/year for 10 years | 10 | 7% | ~$1,425,000 |
| Adult starts at 55, contributes to 65 | $7,000/year for 10 years | 10 | 7% | ~$101,000 |
| Adult starts at 55, contributes to 65 | $8,000/year (with catch-up) for 10 years | 10 | 7% | ~$115,600 |

The difference is staggering. The child's money has 47 extra years to compound. At 7% annual growth, money roughly doubles every 10 years. The child gets 5-6 doublings the adult doesn't.

**But here's the critical detail:** Every dollar in a Roth IRA grows tax-free and comes out tax-free in retirement, per IRS rules. For the child who accumulates $1.4 million, that's $1.4 million in entirely tax-free withdrawals. At a 22% tax rate, the tax savings alone would be worth over $300,000 compared to a taxable account.

## What the Roth IRA Trend Reveals About Retirement Planning

According to the Federal Reserve's 2022 Survey of Consumer Finances:

- **Median retirement savings for households aged 55-64:** $185,000
- **Percentage of Americans aged 55-64 with zero retirement savings:** approximately 13%
- **Average Social Security benefit (2026):** $1,976/month, per SSA data

The parents opening Roth IRAs for their kids understand something that took most of us too long to learn: the tax code rewards people who start early and plan deliberately. The Roth IRA is one of the most powerful tools in the tax code precisely because withdrawals are tax-free, there are no required minimum distributions during the owner's lifetime (unlike traditional IRAs), and contributions can be withdrawn at any time without penalty.

## What This Means If You're 55+

You can't go back in time. But you can still use the same principles — tax-free growth, diversification, and assets that hold value — even with a shorter runway.

**1. Max out your own Roth IRA.** The 2026 limit is $8,000 for those 50 and older (the standard $7,000 plus $1,000 catch-up). If your income exceeds Roth IRA limits ($161,000 single / $240,000 married filing jointly for 2026), look into a Roth conversion or backdoor Roth strategy. You'll pay taxes on the conversion, but all future growth is tax-free.

**2. Consider a Roth conversion ladder.** If you have a traditional IRA or old 401(k), you can convert portions to a Roth each year. According to IRS rules, you pay ordinary income tax on the converted amount, but then everything grows and withdraws tax-free. With the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act provisions scheduled to sunset after 2025, current tax brackets may be lower than what's coming. Converting now could mean paying less tax overall.

**3. Diversify what's inside your retirement accounts.** A self-directed IRA (traditional or Roth) lets you hold assets beyond standard stocks and bonds. Physical gold has averaged 8.3% annual returns over the past 20 years per World Gold Council data. Silver returned an average of 7.2% annually over the same period. Holding precious metals inside a Roth means that growth is entirely tax-free.

**4. Open Roth IRAs for your grandchildren.** If you want to create the same generational advantage for your own family, you can fund a custodial Roth IRA for a grandchild who has earned income. You're limited to gifting up to $18,000 per recipient per year (2026 gift tax exclusion per IRS) without triggering gift tax reporting. The contribution still can't exceed the child's earned income.

## The Bottom Line

The Roth IRA-for-kids trend works because of time and tax-free compounding. Those are real, measurable advantages backed by the math and the tax code. If you're in your 50s or 60s, you have less time but you likely have more capital to work with and access to catch-up contributions, Roth conversions, and self-directed accounts that expand your options.

The underlying principle is the same at any age: put your money in vehicles that grow tax-efficiently, diversify across asset types that don't all move in the same direction, and don't leave your entire retirement in one basket.
